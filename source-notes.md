# Source notes

Own-words working notes on what each source in [BIBLIOGRAPHY.md](BIBLIOGRAPHY.html)
contributes to *The Statistical Turn in Trapped-Ion Noise Diagnostics*, with every formula
rewritten in the Sail's notation (see [GLOSSARY.md](GLOSSARY.html) and the essay §2–§7).

**Why this exists (§0.1).** This is a **derived record** — a paraphrased substitute for the
copyrighted full text, which is never committed. The PDFs are kept untracked in
[`sources/pdfs/`](https://github.com/uwarring82/noise-diagnostics/tree/main/docs/sources) (manifest: [`sources/README.md`](https://github.com/uwarring82/noise-diagnostics/blob/main/docs/sources/README.md)).

**How it was made.** Each note was written from the PDF, then **independently verified
against it** for faithfulness, paraphrase (no verbatim text), and notation consistency. The
verify pass corrected four drafts — Davies (the bath reference state is thermal/KMS, not a
vacuum), Gorini–Kossakowski–Sudarshan (paraphrased a quoted phrase; softened an
"unbounded-generator" overclaim), Hornberger (the collision rate is a momentum operator
whose expectation is our $$\lambda$$, not a state-independent scalar), and Clauset et al.
(nuanced the bias of CCDF vs log-log-histogram fitting). The reviewer-fidelity sign-off
(FR8, §14A.9) remains the human's.


## Ion-trap motional heating & electric-field noise (§1, §5.1)

### brownnutt2015 — Ion-trap measurements of electric-field noise near surfaces

**Relevance.** This is the standard reference for converting a measured motional heating rate into an electric-field PSD $$S_E(\omega_t)$$, and for the catalogue of surface-noise mechanisms the Sail treats as Gaussian-class sources (§5.1). It supplies the $$\dot{\bar n}\leftrightarrow S_E$$ relation, the master-equation framing (§1.1), the empirical frequency/distance/temperature scalings, and the microscopic models (patch potentials, two-level fluctuators, adatom dipoles, adatom diffusion) that predict those scalings. (Their ion charge $$|e|$$ and mass $$m_I$$ are our $$q$$ and $$m$$. Their trap-frequency symbol $$\omega_t$$ is already ours; they note $$\omega_m\equiv\omega_t$$ once in a ring-trap figure caption.)

**Key results (own words).**
- The fluctuating surface field couples linearly to the ion position. Expanding the stray potential about the ion gives an interaction dominated by $$-q\,x\,E_t$$, where $$E_t$$ is the field component along the measured mode axis; higher-order terms are suppressed by powers of (ground-state extent)/(ion–surface distance), i.e. by $$a_0/d\ll1$$, and are dropped. This is exactly the Sail's $$H_\text{int}=-q\,x\,E_x(\mathbf r_0,t)$$.
- Fermi's golden rule turns the ground-to-first-excited transition rate into a sample of the field PSD at the trap frequency. The full expression sums over micromotion sidebands at $$(b/2+j)\Omega_\text{rf}$$ with small weights $$|C_{2j}|\sim(\omega_t/\Omega_\text{rf})^j$$; neglecting micromotion ($$b\Omega_\text{rf}/2\simeq\omega_t$$) collapses this to the single-frequency relation used throughout the review.
- They use a single-sided PSD convention (a factor of 2 in front of the field autocorrelation integral, no negative frequencies). They flag that most theory papers instead use the double-sided definition without that factor of 2 — a relevant correspondence when the Sail compares constants.
- The full dynamics is a damped-oscillator master equation (their Eq. 14): a coherent part plus loss and gain Lindblad terms with rate $$\Gamma$$ and effective bath occupation $$\bar N$$. Near the ground state $$\dot{\bar n}=\Gamma\bar N=\Gamma_h$$; at long times $$\bar n\to\bar N$$, which equals the thermal $$N_\text{th}=1/(e^{\hbar\omega_t/k_BT}-1)$$ for an equilibrium bath. Because typical $$N_\text{th}\sim10^5$$–$$10^7$$, experiments only access the product $$\Gamma\bar N$$, not $$\Gamma$$ and $$\bar N$$ separately. This is the diffusive/Gaussian limit; the review does not develop a jump (compound-Poisson or Lévy) generator — only a Gaussian damped-oscillator $$\mathcal D_G$$-type model.
- Measurement caveat directly relevant to the Sail's non-Gaussian framing: the red/blue-sideband-ratio thermometry assumes a thermal $$P(n)$$ and gives wrong answers when the state is non-thermal — e.g. coherent DAC pickup, or rare background-gas collisions that briefly make the ion very hot. They note that occasional collisions (a few per second or more) inflate the apparent heating rate; this is effectively a sparse-jump (compound-Poisson-like, our $$\lambda\tau\sim1$$) contamination of an otherwise diffusive measurement.
- Empirically they organize data with the power-law ansatz $$S_E\propto\omega^{-\alpha}d^{-\beta}T^{+\gamma}$$, stressing that the ansatz has no a-priori justification and that $$\alpha,\beta,\gamma$$ are at best local exponents that can themselves vary with regime; several mechanisms are not power laws at all.
- Observed scalings: the frequency exponent $$\alpha$$ scatters widely (often $$\approx1$$, i.e. $$1/f$$, but values from $$\sim0$$ to $$\sim6$$ occur), implying multiple distinct noise sources rather than one universal effect. The best-controlled distance measurement (varying one needle trap in situ) gives $$\dot{\bar n}\propto d^{-3.5\pm0.1}$$, i.e. $$\beta=3.5(1)$$; they explicitly warn that the widely quoted "$$d^{-4}$$" is not established by the pooled multi-trap dataset and that the relevant length is a geometry- and model-dependent characteristic distance $$D$$, not the nearest-electrode distance $$d$$.
- Temperature exponent $$\gamma\approx3$$–$$4$$ for many traps (lower in some, e.g. $$\gamma\approx2.1$$ above 70 K in the Chiaverini–Sage data), but $$S_E(T)$$ typically follows $$S_0[1+(T/T_0)^{\gamma}]$$ rather than a pure power, with a low-temperature plateau (a noise floor) below $$\sim30$$ K. Higher room-temperature heating correlates with larger $$\gamma$$.
- Surface mechanisms and their Gaussian-class signatures: (i) Patch potentials — uncorrelated fluctuating voltage patches of area $$A_p$$, coverage $$\sigma_p$$, give $$S_E\propto d^{-4}$$ for patch size small compared to $$d$$; for patches comparable to or larger than $$d$$ the exponent changes (e.g. crossover to $$d^{-2}$$ between parallel plates, where the ion sees an essentially uniform capacitor field). (ii) Two-level fluctuators — an ensemble of double-well defects with a broad (Arrhenius) distribution of relaxation times yields a single-defect Lorentzian that, when averaged, gives $$1/f$$ noise linear in $$T$$ ($$\alpha=1$$, $$\gamma=1$$); the paper treats TLFs as a special class of patch potential. (iii) Adatom dipoles — phonon-driven transitions between bound vibrational states of an adsorbate give a Lorentzian-like single-adatom spectrum (flat below the rate $$\Gamma_0$$, $$\omega^{-2}$$ above), $$d^{-4}$$ distance scaling, thermally activated turn-on at $$T^*=\hbar\nu_{10}/k_B$$, and a high-temperature rise $$\sim T^{2.5}$$ from the multilevel structure. (iv) Adatom diffusion — non-separable space-time correlations give $$\omega^{-2}$$ over a large planar electrode but $$\omega^{-3/2}$$ for a small (needle/patch) electrode, with steep distance scaling ($$d^{-6}$$ perpendicular, $$d^{-8}$$ parallel) and exponential temperature dependence through the diffusion constant ($$S_E\propto D$$).

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\dot{\bar n}\;\simeq\;\frac{q^{2}}{4 m\hbar\omega_t}\,S_E(\omega_t)$$ — their Eq. 12: the standard relation between heating rate and field PSD evaluated at the trap frequency, valid when micromotion sidebands are negligible (exactly the Sail's $$\dot{\bar n}\approx q^2 S_E(\omega_t)/(4m\hbar\omega_t)$$).
- $$\dot{\bar n}\;=\;\frac{q^{2}}{4 m\hbar\omega_t}\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}|C_{2j}|^{2}\,S_E\!\big((b/2+j)\Omega_\text{rf}\big)$$ — their Eq. 10: the full golden-rule rate including micromotion sidebands; the $$j=0$$ term reduces to the line above.
- $$S_E(\omega)\;=\;2\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\! d\tau\,\langle \delta E_t(\tau)\,\delta E_t(0)\rangle\,e^{-i\omega\tau}$$ — their Eq. 11: their single-sided field PSD convention (note the explicit factor of 2; double-sided definitions drop it). Here $$E_t=\delta E_x$$ in the Sail's mode-axis notation.
- $$\dot{\bar n}(t)=-\Gamma\,\bar n(t)+\Gamma\bar N$$, with stationary $$\bar N=N_\text{th}=1/(e^{\hbar\omega_t/k_BT}-1)$$ — their Eqs. 17–18: mean-phonon evolution from the damped-oscillator master equation $$\dot\rho=-i[\,(\omega_t+\delta)a^\dagger a,\rho]+\tfrac{\Gamma}{2}(\bar N+1)\mathcal L[a]+\tfrac{\Gamma}{2}\bar N\,\mathcal L[a^\dagger]$$ (their Eq. 14), the Gaussian-diffusion ($$\mathcal D_G$$-type) generator; near the ground state $$\dot{\bar n}=\Gamma\bar N=\Gamma_h$$.
- $$S_E\;\propto\;\omega^{-\alpha}\,d^{-\beta}\,T^{+\gamma}$$ — their Eq. 30: the empirical scaling ansatz; measured ranges $$\alpha\sim1$$ (with large scatter, $$\sim0$$ to $$\sim6$$), $$\beta=3.5(1)$$ from the one in-situ measurement, $$\gamma\approx3$$–$$4$$ (but really $$S_E\propto S_0[1+(T/T_0)^\gamma]$$ with a low-$$T$$ plateau).
- $$S_E^{(\text{PP})}\simeq s_\eta\,\dfrac{3\sigma_p A_p}{16\pi d^{4}}\,S_V$$ ($$s_\perp=1,\,s_\parallel=1/2$$) — their Eq. 58: patch-potential field PSD for small patches ($$r_c\ll d$$) above a plane, giving the $$d^{-4}$$ law ($$A_p$$ patch area, $$\sigma_p$$ coverage fraction, $$S_V$$ patch-voltage PSD).
- $$\bar S_\mu\simeq \dfrac{\pi k_B T}{4\,\Delta V\,\omega}\,\mu^{2}$$ — their Eq. 68: averaged dipole PSD for thermally activated two-level fluctuators ($$\Delta V$$ the activation-energy interval), showing $$\alpha=1$$ ($$1/f$$) and linear $$T$$ scaling. NB: here $$\mu$$ is the source's per-fluctuator dipole moment, not the Sail's Markov-accessibility $$\mu=\tau/\tau_c$$.

**Domain / caveats.** Everything assumes a single near-ground-state ion in a harmonic trap with compensated stray fields, negligible micromotion-sideband heating, and that the measured state is thermal; under these conditions $$\dot{\bar n}\to\Gamma_h$$ and the $$\dot{\bar n}\leftrightarrow S_E$$ map holds. The review is explicitly Gaussian/equilibrium in spirit — it provides no heavy-tailed or compound-Poisson generator — so for the Sail it anchors the diffusive $$\mathcal D_G$$ limit and the source catalogue, while its sideband-thermometry warnings (coherent pickup, sparse collisions producing non-thermal $$P(n)$$) hint at where the jump/Lévy classes become relevant. The authors caution that pooled multi-trap data do not establish a universal distance law and that the physically meaningful length is a model-dependent characteristic distance $$D$$, not the nearest-electrode $$d$$.

### turchette2000 — Heating of trapped ions from the quantum ground state

**Relevance.** This is the foundational experiment that established anomalous motional heating in ion traps and codified the single-scalar metric $$\dot{\bar n}$$ (mean phonon-gain rate) that the Sail's §1 critiques as insufficient. It fixes the standard linear-coupling model $$H_\text{int}=-qxE_x$$ and the field-PSD relation $$\dot{\bar n}\propto q^2 S_E(\omega_t)$$ that the Sail builds on, while reporting that the heating it sees is far larger than Johnson noise predicts.

**Key results (own words).**
- Heating is modeled as a fluctuating, spatially uniform electric field $$\epsilon(t)$$ coupling to the ion charge; their drive amplitude $$\epsilon(t)$$ corresponds to our $$E_x(\mathbf r_0,t)$$, and their secular frequency $$\omega_m$$ is our $$\omega_t$$.
- Heating is reported as a single number: the rate of growth of the mean phonon occupation, $$\dot{\bar n}$$, derived from first-order perturbation theory as a ground-to-first-excited transition rate proportional to the field PSD at the trap frequency. The measurement assumes the motional state stays thermal.
- Thermometry method: laser/Raman cool to near the ground state ($$\bar n\sim 0$$–$$2$$), insert a variable dark delay $$\tau$$ with no laser interaction, then read out $$\bar n$$ via the red/blue sideband asymmetry; $$\dot{\bar n}$$ is the slope of a linear regression of $$\bar n$$ vs delay $$\tau$$.
- The $$\bar n$$ readout uses the ratio $$R_k$$ of $$k$$-th red- to blue-sideband strengths, which is independent of probe time and valid even outside the Lamb–Dicke regime — but only for a thermal $$P(n)$$. This is a strong-Markovian, Gaussian-class assumption in the Sail's taxonomy: only the first moment $$\bar n$$ is extracted, never the full $$P(n\mid\tau)$$ or its tails.
- Observed magnitudes: trap 1 showed $$\dot{\bar n}\sim 1$$ quantum per millisecond ($$\sim 10^3\,\text{s}^{-1}$$) at $$\omega_t/2\pi=11$$ MHz, drifting upward by roughly 1 quantum/ms per year (attributed to Be deposition) over a multi-year run within a measurement campaign that spanned about five years. These rates are orders of magnitude above the Johnson-noise estimate of $$0.1$$–$$1\,\text{s}^{-1}$$ — hence "anomalous."
- Frequency dependence: across the linear traps the data best fit $$\dot{\bar n}\propto\omega_t^{-2}$$, implying $$S_E(\omega)\propto\omega^{-1}$$ (a flat $$S_E$$ would give $$\dot{\bar n}\propto\omega_t^{-1}$$). They stress this cannot pin down a microscopic mechanism.
- Size dependence is the headline result: controlled same-vacuum comparison (trap 3a vs 3b) gives a heating-rate ratio of $$20\pm6$$, i.e. a scaling exponent $$d^{-3.8\pm0.6}$$ (one rebuilt run even gave $$d^{-12\pm2}$$). This is far steeper than the Johnson-noise prediction $$d^{-2}$$ and consistent with the patch-potential prediction $$d^{-4}$$.
- Cross-experiment comparison with $$^{198}$$Hg$$^+$$ ($$\dot{\bar n}\approx 0.006/$$ms) and $$^{40}$$Ca$$^+$$ ($$\dot{\bar n}\approx 0.005/$$ms), rescaled by $$\omega_t^{-1}$$ and $$m^{-1}$$, is also consistent with $$d^{-4}$$, supporting a microscopic (patch-potential), not bulk-Johnson, noise source. They find no clear dependence on electrode material or static-vs-ponderomotive confinement.
- Appendix derives $$S_E$$ microscopically from the fluctuation–dissipation theorem using a Green tensor $$\mathbf G(\mathbf r_0,\mathbf r;\omega)$$ relating a point dipole in the conductor to the field at the ion — the same Green-tensor object the Sail uses for $$S_E$$.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$H(t)=H_0 - q\,E_x(\mathbf r_0,t)\,x,\qquad H_0=\frac{p^2}{2m}+m\omega_t^2 x^2$$ — their linear coupling Hamiltonian (their Eq. 1, reproduced with its as-printed $$H_0$$); this is the Sail's $$H_\text{int}=-qxE_x(\mathbf r_0,t)$$ (their $$\epsilon(t)\equiv E_x$$, their $$\omega_m\equiv\omega_t$$).
- $$S_E(\omega)\equiv\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}d\tau\,e^{i\omega\tau}\langle E_x(t)E_x(t+\tau)\rangle$$ — the electric-field PSD; matches the Sail's $$S_E(\omega)$$.
- $$\dot{\bar n}\approx \frac{q^2}{4m\hbar\omega_t}\,S_E(\omega_t)$$ — the central heating-rate relation (their Eq. 4, neglecting the small rf-sideband cross-coupling term $$\propto\omega_t^2/\Omega_T^2\sim10^{-4}$$); identical to the Sail's key relation $$\dot{\bar n}\approx q^2 S_E(\omega_t)/(4m\hbar\omega_t)$$. Their rf drive frequency $$\Omega_T$$ has no Sail counterpart.
- $$R_k\equiv\frac{I_k^{\text{rsb}}}{I_k^{\text{bsb}}},\qquad \bar n=\frac{(R_k)^{1/k}}{1-(R_k)^{1/k}}$$ — sideband-asymmetry thermometry returning $$\bar n$$ assuming a thermal $$P(n)=\bar n^{\,n}/(1+\bar n)^{n+1}$$ ($$R_k$$ is their red/blue sideband strength ratio, independent of probe time and valid even outside the Lamb–Dicke regime).
- $$\dot{\bar n}_R=\frac{q^2 k_B T R(\omega_t)}{m\hbar\omega_t d^2}\propto d^{-2},\qquad \dot{\bar n}_P=\frac{3q^2 C r_p^2 S_V(\omega_t)}{16 m\hbar\omega_t d^4}\propto d^{-4}$$ — Johnson-noise (Eq. 5) vs patch-potential (Eq. 7) heating, with $$d$$ the ion–electrode distance; the measured $$\sim d^{-4}$$ favors patches.
- $$S_{E_i}(\mathbf r,\omega)=\frac{2k_B T}{\omega}\sum_j \Im\,G_{ij}(\mathbf r,\mathbf r,\omega)$$ — fluctuation–dissipation form of the field PSD via the Green tensor $$\mathbf G(\mathbf r_0,\mathbf r;\omega)$$ (their $$G_{ij}$$).

**Domain / caveats.** Everything rests on a thermal-state, mean-occupation picture: only $$\bar n$$ (hence $$\dot{\bar n}$$) is measured, never the shape of $$P(n\mid\tau)$$, so by construction the data cannot distinguish Gaussian-diffusive from compound-Poisson or Lévy-type jump dynamics — exactly the single-scalar limitation the Sail seeks to refine. The linearized perturbative $$\dot{\bar n}\propto S_E(\omega_t)$$ relation assumes weak, stationary, on-resonance field noise (which, in the Sail's taxonomy, is implicitly Markov-accessible, $$\mu\gg1$$); the size-scaling exponents vary widely between traps/runs ($$d^{-3.8}$$ to $$d^{-12}$$), so the $$d^{-4}$$ patch conclusion is suggestive rather than definitive.

### deslauriers2006 — Scaling and suppression of anomalous heating in ion traps

**Relevance.** Supplies the empirical ion–electrode distance scaling and temperature-suppression of the anomalous heating rate that the Sail uses to anchor the Gaussian-class distance/temperature trends (§5.1) and to benchmark physical noise models in Pillar-2 (§7). Provides the measured exponents and the model interpretation (patch size vs. electrode spacing).

**Key results (own words).**
- They use a double-needle rf trap whose tip-to-tip gap $$2z_0$$ (here $$z_0$$ = ion-to-electrode distance) is tunable in situ, letting them vary electrode proximity for a single trap without confounds from changing materials/geometry. Distances span $$z_0 \approx 23$$–$$250\,\mu$$m with a single $$^{111}$$Cd$$^+$$ ion.
- The heating rate $$\dot{\bar n}$$ is obtained from the slope of the linearly-growing mean phonon number $$\bar n(\tau)$$ measured after a variable delay $$\tau$$ following ground-state cooling (sideband-asymmetry thermometry). So $$\dot{\bar n}$$ here is the Gaussian-class linear-diffusion rate.
- Distance scaling (the central result the Sail draws on): at fixed $$\omega_t/2\pi = 2.07$$ MHz, $$\dot{\bar n} \propto z_0^{-3.47 \pm 0.16}$$. (Their distance symbol $$z_0$$ maps to the Sail's electrode distance $$d$$.)
- Interpretation of the exponent: a single localized patch (size $$\ll z_0$$) gives $$z_0^{-4}$$; uniformly distributed microscopic patches give somewhere between $$z_0^{-2}$$ and $$z_0^{-4}$$; fully correlated noise across the electrodes (e.g. Johnson/applied-voltage noise) gives $$z_0^{-2}$$. The observed $$\approx z_0^{-3.5}$$ rules out correlated/circuit noise and is consistent with many uncorrelated patches smaller than $$z_0$$.
- Temperature suppression: cooling the needle tips from 300 K to about 150 K (factor-of-two temperature drop) lowers $$\dot{\bar n}$$ by roughly an order of magnitude — a strongly nonlinear, super-linear dependence, far steeper than the modest factor expected from Johnson noise alone. They suggest a thermally activated process with a possible threshold, hinting that cooling to 77 K might quench anomalous heating entirely.
- Even cooled, the measured rate sits about two orders of magnitude above the predicted thermal (Johnson) circuit-noise floor, so the dominant noise is anomalous (patch-potential) rather than circuit-resistive.
- Frequency scaling (secondary, but constrains $$S_E$$): at fixed $$z_0 = 103\,\mu$$m, $$\dot{\bar n} \propto \omega_t^{-1.8 \pm 0.4}$$, implying the field PSD scales as $$S_E(\omega_t) \propto \omega_t^{-0.8 \pm 0.4}$$ over their range.
- Field-noise model: voltage noise $$S_V$$ on the electrodes converts to field noise at the ion via $$S_E = (\epsilon/2z_0)^2 S_V$$ with geometric factor $$\epsilon \approx 0.7$$; the $$1/z_0^2$$ here is the correlated-noise baseline that the data exceed.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\dot{\bar n} \propto d^{-3.47 \pm 0.16}$$ — measured distance scaling of the heating rate with ion–electrode distance $$d$$ (their $$z_0$$), at fixed $$\omega_t$$; consistent with uncorrelated sub-$$d$$ patch potentials.
- $$\dot{\bar n} \propto \omega_t^{-1.8 \pm 0.4}$$, equivalently $$S_E(\omega_t) \propto \omega_t^{-0.8 \pm 0.4}$$ — frequency scaling of rate and electric-field PSD (their $$\omega_z = \omega_t$$).
- $$\dot{\bar n} = \frac{q^2}{4 m \hbar \omega_t}\left[\, S_E(\omega_t) + \frac{\omega_t^2}{2\Omega^2}\, S_E(\Omega \pm \omega_t)\,\right]$$ — heating rate from the field PSD at the secular frequency plus a small rf-sideband cross term ($$\Omega$$ = rf drive frequency, $$\Omega/2\pi = 29$$ MHz here; their $$e \to q$$, $$\omega_z \to \omega_t$$). The Sail's core relation $$\dot{\bar n} \approx q^2 S_E(\omega_t)/(4 m \hbar \omega_t)$$ is the leading term.
- $$S_E(\omega) = \left(\frac{\epsilon}{2d}\right)^2 S_V(\omega),\qquad \epsilon \approx 0.7$$ — conversion from electrode voltage noise to field noise at the ion; the explicit $$1/d^2$$ is the correlated-noise (Johnson) lower bound, scaling as $$\dot{\bar n}\propto d^{-2}$$.
- $$\dot{\bar n}_{\text{thermal}} \sim \left(\frac{200}{z_0}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\omega_t/2\pi}{\text{MHz}}\right)^{-1}\ \text{s}^{-1}\quad(300\,\text{K})$$ — estimated Johnson-noise heating floor ($$z_0$$ in $$\mu$$m); about a factor of 6 lower at 150 K (including a factor-of-3 drop in tungsten resistivity). The data lie well above this.

**Domain / caveats.** Single-ion, single trap (needle/point-electrode geometry, $$^{111}$$Cd$$^+$$, $$\omega_t/2\pi \approx 2$$–5 MHz, room and $$\sim$$150 K only — not true cryogenic); exponents are fitted power laws with stated uncertainties, and the $$\approx z_0^{-3.5}$$ value cannot pin down the patch-size distribution exactly. The temperature points are sparse (three cold measurements on one ion over a 6-hour period), so the "thermal activation/threshold" reading is suggestive, not quantitative. All scalings concern the Gaussian linear-diffusion rate; the paper says nothing about jump statistics, heavy tails, or non-Markovian ($$\mu \lesssim 1$$) regimes.

## Open quantum systems: master-equation structure (§3, §5)

### davies1974 — Markovian master equations

**Relevance.** This is the rigorous foundation for treating an open system's evolution as a Markovian generator: it proves that bath memory drops out in a weak-coupling / van-Hove limit, and pins the validity of that limit to fast decay of the bath two-point correlation function. The Sail uses it to justify the generator split $$\dot\rho = -i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]+\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$ and to motivate the Markov-accessibility gate $$\mu=\tau/\tau_c\gg1$$: "Markovian" requires the interrogation/relaxation time to dwarf the bath correlation time $$\tau_c$$.

**Key results (own words).**
- Starting from an exact, reversible projection-operator (Nakajima–Zwanzig) equation, Davies isolates the integral memory kernel and shows it becomes negligible in the limit where the system–bath coupling strength tends to zero. (His coupling constant is written $$\lambda$$ in the paper; this is the perturbation strength, NOT the Sail's event rate $$\lambda$$ — call it $$\lambda_c$$ for clarity.)
- To compensate for the ever-slower relaxation as $$\lambda_c\to0$$, he rescales time as $$\tau=\lambda_c^2 t$$ (the van Hove timescale). On this rescaled clock the dynamics converges to a one-parameter contraction semigroup $$Y_\tau=\exp(K^\sharp\tau)$$, i.e. memoryless exponential decay generated by a fixed superoperator $$K$$ (his Theorem 2.1).
- The limiting generator is the bath-averaged second-order kernel $$K=\int_0^\infty P_0 A\,\hat A\,P_0\,dt$$ — the standard time-averaged Born term — and it commutes with the free evolution (secular/rotating-wave averaging is built in via the $$\sharp$$ operation, Eqs. 2.16–2.17, with $$K^\sharp U_t = U_t K^\sharp$$ in Eq. 2.22).
- The decisive technical hypothesis is integrability of the bath correlation function: the limit and the neglect of memory hold provided the bath two-point function $$h_1(t)$$ satisfies $$\int_0^\infty|h_1(t)|\,(1+|t|)^\epsilon\,dt<\infty$$ for some $$\epsilon>0$$ (Theorem 3.5, Eq. 3.39). Plain integrability $$\int|h_1|\,dt<\infty$$ already gives existence of $$K$$ (Theorems 3.1 and 3.4); the extra weight controls the error terms uniformly (Eq. 2.39).
- Physically this means the bath correlations must decay faster than any power with a finite first absolute moment — i.e. a finite, well-defined correlation time $$\tau_c$$. A bath with a sharply finite $$\tau_c$$ is exactly the regime $$\mu=\tau/\tau_c\gg1$$ where the Markovian master equation is licensed; a slowly/heavy-tailed-decaying correlation function violates Eq. (3.39) and the Markov reduction can fail.
- Applied to an $$N$$-level system coupled to an infinite free heat bath through a single linear system operator, he gets an explicit generator in Lindblad form with separate emission, absorption, and Lamb-shift pieces (Eqs. 4.9–4.10), and the bath spectral weights obey detailed balance $$a(\omega)=e^{-\beta\omega}e(\omega)$$, driving the system to its Gibbs state at the bath temperature.
- The irreversibility is shown to originate in the initial conditions — the bath being prepared in its (thermal) reference state, combined with the projection structure — not in any coarse-graining or ad hoc averaging (his remark 5): a clean separation of the system from a memoryless environment.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\tau = \lambda_c^2\, t$$ — van Hove time rescaling; $$\lambda_c$$ = system–bath coupling strength (their $$\lambda$$), distinct from the Sail's event rate $$\lambda$$. The Markovian generator lives on this slow clock.
- $$\dot\rho = K^\sharp\rho,\qquad \rho(\tau)=e^{K^\sharp\tau}\rho(0)$$ — the weak-coupling limit yields a memoryless semigroup; this $$K^\sharp$$ is the precursor of the Sail's full generator $$-i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]+\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$ (Davies' integrable-bath, single-linear-coupling case lands in the Gaussian/Lindblad part $$-i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]$$, with no Lévy/jump term).
- $$K = \int_0^\infty P_0\,A\,\hat A\,P_0\;dt = \int_0^\infty \langle E_x(t)E_x(0)\rangle\,(\cdots)\,dt$$ — the limit generator is a time integral of the bath force/field autocorrelation (his interaction $$A$$ maps onto our $$H_\text{int}=-qxE_x$$, with the system operator $$Q\leftrightarrow x$$ and the bath field $$\Phi\leftrightarrow E_x$$); the integral is finite precisely when the correlation decays on a time $$\tau_c$$.
- $$\int_0^\infty |h_1(t)|\,(1+|t|)^\epsilon\,dt < \infty \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \tau_c<\infty,\ \ \mu=\tau/\tau_c\gg1$$ — the validity condition: bath correlation function $$h_1(t)$$ (our $$S_E(\omega)$$ is essentially its Fourier transform) must decay with a finite weighted moment, i.e. a finite bath correlation time, which is the microscopic content of the Markov-accessibility gate.
- $$K^\sharp(\rho)=\sum_{\omega\ge0} e(\omega)\big(A_{-\omega}\rho A_\omega-\tfrac12\{A_\omega A_{-\omega},\rho\}\big)+\sum_{\omega>0} a(\omega)\big(\cdots\big)+\sum_\omega i s(\omega)[A_{-\omega}A_\omega,\rho]$$ — explicit Lindblad/GKLS dissipator with emission rate $$e(\omega)$$, absorption rate $$a(\omega)$$, and Lamb shift $$s(\omega)$$; $$a(\omega)=e^{-\beta\omega}e(\omega)$$ enforces detailed balance and relaxation to the Gibbs state.

**Domain / caveats.** Strict validity is asymptotic in vanishing coupling $$\lambda_c\to0$$ on the rescaled time $$\tau=\lambda_c^2 t$$, for a finite-dimensional system whose free Hamiltonian has discrete spectrum and a bath with a fast-decaying (finite-moment) correlation function $$h_1(t)$$; the limits $$\lambda_c\to0$$ and $$t\to\infty$$ are not interchanged here (uniformity in time is not established in this paper), so the result governs the generator/timescale-separation regime, not arbitrarily long times. It does not by itself produce or bound non-Gaussian jump ($$\mathcal{J}_P$$) or Lévy contributions — those lie outside the integrable-correlation, single-linear-coupling setting Davies treats.

### gks1976 — Completely positive dynamical semigroups of N-level systems

**Relevance.** This paper fixes the algebraic structure of the dissipator that the Sail's master equation must respect: it proves that the most general generator of a completely positive, trace-preserving Markovian semigroup on a finite-dimensional system splits into a Hamiltonian commutator plus a dissipative term built from a *positive-semidefinite* coefficient matrix. Together with Lindblad's infinite-dimensional companion result, this is the "G" half of the GKSL form underlying the Sail's $$\mathcal{D}_G + \mathcal{J}_P$$ split (§5): complete positivity is exactly what forces our Gaussian-diffusion and compound-Poisson superoperators to be physically admissible (rates and jump densities nonnegative).

**Key results (own words).**
- They work with an $$N$$-level system, identifying states with $$N\times N$$ density matrices, and ask for the generator $$L$$ of a one-parameter semigroup of trace-preserving, completely positive maps acting on $$\rho$$ (their $$p$$). Their $$L$$ is our generator on the right-hand side of $$\dot\rho = L\rho$$.
- "Completely positive" is strictly stronger than positive: the map must stay positive even when tensored with the identity on an arbitrary ancilla of any size $$n$$. They give an explicit positive-but-not-2-positive counterexample (Appendix A) to show the distinction is real, so positivity alone is not enough.
- Physical justification for demanding complete positivity: if the open system $$S$$ is a subsystem of a larger isolated $$S+R$$ evolving unitarily from an initial product state, the reduced map on $$S$$ is automatically completely positive (Kraus); this property survives the weak-coupling and singular-reservoir (short bath-correlation-time) limits that produce a Markovian semigroup. In Sail terms, complete positivity is the residue of "there exists an underlying unitary bath model," and it is preserved precisely in the regime $$\tau_c \to 0$$ (large Markov-accessibility $$\mu \gg 1$$).
- Main structural theorem (their Thm 2.2): any such generator can be written as a Hamiltonian part plus a dissipator whose coefficients form a complex *positive* matrix $$\{c_{ij}\}$$ in a fixed traceless operator basis $$\{F_i\}$$. Conversely, any $$L$$ of that form with $$\{c_{ij}\}\succeq 0$$ generates a completely positive trace-preserving semigroup. The Hamiltonian $$H$$ appearing here is generally renormalized and need not equal the bare system Hamiltonian $$H_0$$ (their Remark; a Lamb-type shift), and $$H$$ is fixed uniquely by the traceless condition $$\mathrm{tr}(H)=0$$.
- Positivity of $$\{c_{ij}\}$$ is the crucial constraint. After diagonalizing it (rotating the basis to $$\{G_p\}$$) the dissipator becomes a sum over channels each weighted by an eigenvalue $$\lambda_p \ge 0$$. These nonnegative eigenvalues play the role of dissipation/jump rates; for the Sail this is what guarantees that diffusion coefficients in $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ and the jump-event rate $$\lambda$$ / jump density $$\nu(\Delta p)$$ in $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ are nonnegative.
- For $$N=2$$ they specialize to the Bloch equations: the polarization components $$M_i(t)=\mathrm{tr}(\rho_t F_i)$$ relax with inverse relaxation times $$\gamma_i = 1/T_i$$ toward an equilibrium, and complete positivity imposes explicit triangle-like inequalities among the $$\gamma_i$$ — in their words, none of the three relaxation times may greatly exceed the sum of the other two. This is the finite-level analogue of consistency constraints the Sail's reduced dynamics must satisfy.
- The derivation is purely algebraic and exact in finite dimension (no perturbative truncation): it is a characterization theorem, not an approximation. The continuous-variable trapped-ion problem the Sail treats is infinite-dimensional, so the literal GKS theorem applies to a truncated/finite phonon basis; the infinite-dimensional (continuous-mode) version is Lindblad's job.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\dot\rho = L\rho = -i[H,\rho] + \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^{N^2-1} c_{ij}\big([F_i,\rho F_j^\dagger] + [F_i\rho, F_j^\dagger]\big),\qquad \{c_{ij}\}\succeq 0$$ — their Eq. (2.3), the general completely-positive trace-preserving generator; here $$\{F_i\}$$ is a traceless orthonormal operator basis ($$\mathrm{tr}(F_i^\dagger F_j)=\delta_{ij}$$, $$\mathrm{tr}(F_i)=0$$), $$H=H^\dagger$$ with $$\mathrm{tr}(H)=0$$, and $$c_{ij}$$ is their notation for the channel-coefficient (Kossakowski) matrix. This is the "G"/Gaussian-Lindblad backbone of the Sail's $$-i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]+\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$; the requirement $$\{c_{ij}\}\succeq 0$$ is complete positivity.
- $$\dot\rho = -i[H,\rho] + \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{p} \lambda_p\big([G_p,\rho G_p^\dagger] + [G_p\rho, G_p^\dagger]\big),\qquad \lambda_p \ge 0$$ — diagonalized form: rotating $$\{F_i\}\to\{G_p\}$$ to diagonalize $$c_{ij}$$ gives independent dissipation channels with nonnegative weights $$\lambda_p$$ (their $$\lambda_p$$, the eigenvalues of $$\{c_{ij}\}$$). Each nonnegative $$\lambda_p$$ is a rate; this is exactly where the Sail reads off that its heating/jump rates cannot be negative.
- $$\dot M_i = \sum_{j,k}\epsilon_{ijk} h_j (M_k - M_k^0) - \gamma_i (M_i - M_i^0)$$ — their two-level Eq. (3.2), the Bloch equations; $$M_i$$ are polarization components, $$h_j$$ the (renormalized) field, and $$\gamma_i = 1/T_i$$ inverse relaxation times.
- $$\gamma_i \le \gamma_j + \gamma_k$$ (equivalently $$\kappa_1,\kappa_2,\kappa_3 \ge 0$$ with $$\kappa_i = \sum_j\gamma_j - 2\gamma_i$$) — the complete-positivity triangle inequality on the two-level relaxation rates: it is the $$\gamma$$-form of their Eq. (3.5) (from condition set 3.4), expressing that no single relaxation time can greatly exceed the sum of the other two. Note: their $$\kappa_i$$ here are auxiliary rate combinations, unrelated to the Sail's cumulants $$\kappa_3,\kappa_4$$.

**Domain / caveats.** Strictly finite-dimensional ($$N$$-level) and Markovian (semigroup, time-homogeneous, memoryless) — it presumes the bath correlation time is negligible relative to system timescales (the $$\tau_c\to 0$$ / weak-coupling regime, Sail's $$\mu\gg1$$). It supplies no jump/diffusion split of its own: the Lévy–Khintchine decomposition into $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ (Gaussian) versus $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ (compound-Poisson) and the heavy-tailed Lévy regime are not in this paper; GKS only guarantees the overall generator is of completely-positive form with a positive coefficient matrix. The continuous-mode oscillator the Sail actually models needs the infinite-dimensional (Lindblad) extension.

### lindblad1976 — On the generators of quantum dynamical semigroups

**Relevance.** This is the structural theorem the Sail's master-equation split specialises: it proves that the most general bounded generator of a norm-continuous, completely positive (trace-preserving) Markovian semigroup is a Hamiltonian commutator plus a dissipator built from "jump" operators. Lindblad himself flags the result as the quantum analogue of the Lévy–Khinchin formula, which is exactly the Gaussian-diffusion-plus-compound-Poisson decomposition the Sail uses for the noise generator.

**Key results (own words).**
- The setting is open-system dynamics: a one-parameter family of maps on the system observables that is completely positive (CP, in the sense of Stinespring), identity-preserving, and forms a semigroup; the Markov/semigroup property is argued to hold in the intermediate regime where the system's timescale far exceeds the bath relaxation time but stays below the recurrence time — directly the Sail's Markov-accessibility condition (here their "bath relaxation time" is our correlation time $$\tau_c$$, so the regime is our $$\mu = \tau/\tau_c \gg 1$$).
- Complete positivity is the essential extra demand beyond mere positivity: it is required so the dynamics stays physical when the system is tensored with an arbitrary spectator, and it is what forces the rigid Lindblad form. For classical (commutative) systems positivity already implies CP, so the constraint is purely quantum.
- Restricting to a **bounded** generator (norm-continuous semigroup) is the price for getting an explicit canonical form; Lindblad notes many physical applications have unbounded generators and this case is left open. This matters for the Sail because its trap/heating generators are typically unbounded, so the theorem applies in spirit/structure rather than verbatim.
- The generator's deviation from unitary (Hamiltonian) dynamics is captured by a "dissipation function" $$D(L;X,Y)=L(X^\dagger Y)-L(X^\dagger)Y-X^\dagger L(Y)$$; the Hamiltonian part is exactly the piece with $$D=0$$, so $$H$$ is fixed only modulo the dissipative content — the Hamiltonian/dissipator split is not unique.
- Main result (Theorem 2): every such generator decomposes into a commutator term plus a sum of jump terms built from operators $$V_j$$ (their notation; these are our jump operators). Each $$V_j$$ contributes both a "sandwich" gain term and an anticommutator loss term that preserves the trace/identity.
- Convex-cone structure (§5): the generators modulo Hamiltonians form a convex cone whose **extreme rays (pure generators)** are the building blocks. Two named special cases of pure generators are singled out — **Gaussian** semigroups (built from a single self-adjoint $$V$$) and **Poisson** semigroups (built from a single unitary $$V$$). This is precisely the Sail's $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ (diffusive) versus $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ (jump) dichotomy.
- The Lévy–Khinchin analogy is explicit: classically the generator of a convolution semigroup splits into three pure pieces — a Gaussian (diffusion) generator, a translation (drift) generator, and an integral over Poisson (jump) generators; Lindblad shows the quantum generator (4.2)/(4.3) is the sum over pure elements that generalises this. The quantum extreme rays are *more general* than the classical ones but contain the Gaussian and Poisson generators as special cases.
- Equation (4.3) is stated to be the most general time-homogeneous, bounded Markovian master equation in the Schrödinger picture — i.e. the canonical form into which empirically all such master equations rearrange (allowing for unbounded operators). This is the equation the Sail's $$\dot\rho$$ specialises.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\dot\rho \;=\; -i[H_0,\rho] \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_j\Big([V_j\rho,\,V_j^\dagger] + [V_j,\,\rho\,V_j^\dagger]\Big)$$ — Lindblad's Schrödinger-picture canonical generator (his Eq. 4.3, with $$H\to H_0$$); the second term is the dissipator with jump operators $$V_j$$. Equivalent to the familiar $$\sum_j\big(V_j\rho V_j^\dagger-\tfrac12\{V_j^\dagger V_j,\rho\}\big)$$.
- $$\dot\rho \;=\; -i[H_0,\rho] \;+\; \mathcal{D}_G[\rho] \;+\; \mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$ — the Sail mapping: the commutator is the coherent part; $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ collects the **Gaussian/diffusive** pure generators (his self-adjoint case below, our continuous-diffusion superoperator), and $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ collects the **Poisson jump** pure generators (his unitary case below, our compound-Poisson superoperator with kicks $$\Delta p$$ and displacements $$U_{\Delta p}=e^{-i\Delta p\,x/\hbar}$$).
- $$\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]\;\leftrightarrow\;$$ Gaussian semigroup generator $$L(X)=VXV-\tfrac12\{V^2,X\}$$ with **$$V$$ self-adjoint** (his §5) — the diffusive piece; taking $$V\propto x$$ or $$V\propto p$$ gives momentum/position diffusion, i.e. the heating term that drives $$\dot{\bar n}\approx q^2 S_E(\omega_t)/(4 m\hbar\omega_t)$$.
- $$\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]\;\leftrightarrow\;$$ Poisson semigroup generator $$L(X)=V^\dagger X V - X$$ with **$$V$$ unitary** (his §5) — the jump piece; a unitary $$V=U_{\Delta p}$$ produces a discrete momentum kick, and summing/integrating over kicks with density $$\nu(\Delta p)$$ rebuilds the compound-Poisson $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ (their unitary $$V$$ is our $$U_{\Delta p}$$, their rate is our $$\lambda$$).
- $$D(L;X,Y)=L(X^\dagger Y)-L(X^\dagger)Y-X^\dagger L(Y)$$ — the dissipation function; $$D\equiv0$$ singles out the purely Hamiltonian (reversible) part, isolating the noise generator from $$-i[H_0,\rho]$$.

**Domain / caveats.** Result is exact only for **bounded** generators (norm-continuous semigroups) of completely-positive, identity-preserving maps; unbounded cases (the Sail's actual trap+field generators) are not covered by the explicit form but inherit its structure. The semigroup/Markov assumption corresponds to the Sail's $$\mu=\tau/\tau_c\gg1$$ regime; the Hamiltonian-vs-dissipator and the Gaussian-vs-jump splits are physically the right decomposition but are not unique at the level of individual $$H,V_j$$, so the Sail should treat $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ and $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ as the invariant (equivalence-class) content, not the specific operators.

### holevo1996 — Covariant quantum Markovian evolutions

**Relevance.** This is the structure theorem the Sail invokes to justify writing a translation/shift-covariant Lindblad generator as a Lévy–Khintchine sum of a Gaussian (diffusion) piece and a compound-Poisson (jump) piece, $$\mathcal{D}_G+\mathcal{J}_P$$, with the jump part controlled by a Lévy measure that is our jump-size density $$\nu(\Delta p)$$. It proves that covariance alone forces this decomposition and identifies exactly which terms are allowed.

**Key results (own words).**
- For a dynamical semigroup that is covariant under the Weyl (canonical-shift) group, the generator is fixed by a single scalar function $$\lambda(z)$$ on phase space that is "conditionally positive definite" with $$\lambda(0)\le 0$$; equivalently the semigroup is a noise-averaged conjugation by Weyl displacements driven by a classical process with stationary independent increments. So covariance $$\Rightarrow$$ the noise is a (generally non-Gaussian) Lévy process.
- The classical multidimensional Lévy–Khintchine formula then splits $$\lambda(z)$$ into three additive parts: a linear drift term, a non-negative quadratic (Gaussian/diffusion) term, and an integral over a positive Lévy measure $$\nu$$ of "jump" contributions. This is the exact origin of our $$H_0$$ (drift) + $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ (quadratic) + $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ (Lévy-measure integral) split.
- Translating into the master equation, the generator is the sum of a Hamiltonian commutator, double-commutator (diffusive) terms built from canonical operators, and an integral $$\int(U\rho U^\dagger-\rho)\,\nu(dz)$$ where $$U$$ runs over Weyl displacements — i.e. a continuum of unitary "kick" channels weighted by the jump density. Holevo notes this generalizes the finite-dimensional Gaussian-plus-Poissonian generator (his "$$[L,[L,X]]$$" Gaussian part and "$$V^\dagger XV-X$$" Poissonian part) to unbounded operators and an integral over all jump magnitudes.
- For full Galilean covariance (zero spin), he proves the reservoir reduces to a *classical* Lévy noise on phase space added to free motion; no purely quantum (non-classical) noise survives, and "explosion" (probability leaking out of phase space) cannot occur. This is the clean, fully-rigorous case.
- Dropping space-shift/rotation covariance and keeping only *boost* covariance (covariance under momentum shifts — the relevant symmetry for a particle in an arbitrary potential, hence a trapped ion) gives a much richer generator: still Lévy–Khintchine in form (a "continuous" $$\mathcal{L}_1$$, a "big-jumps" $$\mathcal{L}_2$$, and a "small-jumps" $$\mathcal{L}_3$$ component), but now position-dependent and not reducible to classical noise. Holevo flags this case as only formally established.
- Restricting the boost-covariant generator to functions of position $$f(Q)$$ recovers a classical sub-Markov generator with a drift, a constant diffusion matrix, and a jump kernel $$\nu(dx\mid Q)$$ — literally a Fokker–Planck-plus-jump (Kolmogorov–Feller) operator. This is the bridge from the quantum master equation to a classical drift–diffusion–jump process for an observable, which is what the Sail needs to connect $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ to a measurable jump-size density.
- Unitality / trace preservation corresponds exactly to $$\lambda(0)=0$$; a non-unital generator ($$\lambda(0)<0$$) means probability loss, i.e. the classical process can "explode." Uniqueness of the semigroup reduces to non-explosion of the associated classical Markov process.
- The decomposition is non-unique: small-jump compensators and constant shifts in the jump operators can be moved between the Hamiltonian, diffusion, and jump parts. Practically, this means the $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ vs $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ split is a modeling choice that must be fixed by a convention (e.g. a cutoff $$\varepsilon$$ separating small from big jumps).

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\lambda(z)-\lambda(0)=i\,b(z)-\tfrac12 a(z)+\!\int_{z'\neq0}\!\big[e^{i\Delta(z,z')}-1-i\Delta(z,z')\,\mathbf 1_\varepsilon(z')\big]\,\nu(dz')$$ — the (his) Lévy–Khintchine symbol of the covariant generator: $$b$$ = drift, $$a\ge0$$ = Gaussian/diffusion quadratic form, $$\nu$$ = Lévy jump measure. In our split the $$a$$-term becomes $$\mathcal{D}_G$$, the $$\nu$$-integral becomes $$\mathcal{J}_P$$; $$\Delta$$ is the symplectic form (his $$D$$).
- $$\dot\rho=-i[H_0,\rho]+\underbrace{\sum_{j,k}c_{jk}\,\big(L_j\rho L_k^\dagger-\tfrac12\{L_k^\dagger L_j,\rho\}\big)}_{\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]}+\underbrace{\int\big(U_{\Delta p}\,\rho\,U_{\Delta p}^\dagger-\rho\big)\,\nu(d\Delta p)}_{\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]}$$ — his Eq. (2.12)/(3.15)/(4.7) recast in our symbols: a Hamiltonian term, a Gaussian diffusion superoperator (which in his canonical form appears as the double-commutator $$-\tfrac12\sum_j\{a_{PP}[P_j,[P_j,\rho]]+2a_{PQ}[P_j,[Q_j,\rho]]+a_{QQ}[Q_j,[Q_j,\rho]]\}$$ built from $$x,P$$), and a compound-Poisson jump term. Here $$U_{\Delta p}=\exp[-i\Delta p\,x/\hbar]$$ is the momentum-kick Weyl displacement (his $$W(z)$$ / $$U_x=e^{-ix\cdot P}$$; *their position-shift operator $$U_x$$ is the dual of our momentum-kick $$U_{\Delta p}$$ under $$x\leftrightarrow P$$*), and $$\nu(d\Delta p)$$ is his Lévy measure $$\nu(dz)$$ / $$m(dx)$$ = our jump-size density.
- $$\int_{\Delta p\neq0}\!\big(|\Delta p|^2\,\mathbf 1_\varepsilon+\mathbf 1_{|\Delta p|\ge\varepsilon}\big)\,\nu(d\Delta p)<\infty$$ — his Lévy integrability condition (2.9)/(3.14): small jumps must be square-summable, big jumps merely finite in rate. This is exactly the admissibility constraint a heavy-tailed $$\nu(\Delta p)\propto|\Delta p|^{-1-\alpha}$$ ($$0<\alpha<2$$) satisfies, so Lévy-type (stable) jump kernels are allowed generators.
- $$\mathcal{L}[f(Q)]=K_0 f+\sum_k b_k\,\partial_{Q_k}f+\tfrac12\sum_{l,k}\sigma_{lk}\,\partial_{Q_l}\partial_{Q_k}f+\!\int\!\Big[f(Q+x)-f(Q)-\mathbf 1_\varepsilon\!\sum_k x_k\partial_{Q_k}f\Big]\,\nu(dx\mid Q)$$ — his Eq. (4.15): the boost-covariant generator restricted to position observables is a classical drift–diffusion–jump (Kolmogorov–Feller) operator with $$Q$$-dependent drift $$b(Q)$$, constant diffusion matrix $$\sigma$$, and state-dependent jump intensity $$\nu(dx\mid Q)$$ (his $$m(dx\mid Q)$$). This is the classical limit that lets $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ be read off as an observable-level compound-Poisson jump process.

**Domain / caveats.** The fully rigorous, "noise is necessarily classical, no explosion" results hold for full Galilean (Weyl + boost + rotation) covariance with zero spin and unbounded but well-controlled generators. The boost-only case — the one closest to a trapped ion in a potential, and the one giving genuinely quantum, position-dependent jump structure — is developed by Holevo only at a formal level: existence/uniqueness, the precise small-jump compensation, and non-explosion are flagged as open. The Sail should treat the $$\mathcal{D}_G+\mathcal{J}_P$$ Lévy–Khintchine split as the *allowed structural form* of any shift-covariant generator (including Lévy-type $$\nu$$), not as a derivation of the specific heating kernel, which still requires the physical $$S_E(\omega)$$/$$S_F(\omega)$$ input.

### breuer2007 — The Theory of Open Quantum Systems (Oxford, 2007)

**Relevance.** This is the textbook the Sail imports its master-equation and decoherence formalism from: Chapter 3 supplies the Markovian (Lindblad/GKSL) generator that the Sail's $$-i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]$$ piece specializes, and Chapter 4 supplies the position-localization master equation and decoherence-rate picture that ground the Sail's reading of momentum-kick / phonon-heating dynamics as decay of off-diagonal coherences.

**Key results (own words).**
- Open-system reduced dynamics: split the total Hamiltonian into system, bath, and interaction, $$H=H_S+H_B+H_I$$, and obtain the reduced state $$\rho_S=\mathrm{tr}_B\,\rho$$ by tracing the unitarily-evolved total state over the bath; the exact reduced equation is non-local in time (the explicit memory-kernel form is deferred to Part IV).
- When the bath correlation time is much shorter than the system's evolution time scale, memory is negligible and the reduced dynamics becomes a quantum dynamical semigroup $$V(t_1)V(t_2)=V(t_1+t_2)$$ — this is exactly the Sail's Markov-accessible regime $$\mu=\tau/\tau_c\gg1$$. In Sail terms, "$$\tau_c$$ short" $$\Leftrightarrow$$ "$$\mu\gg1$$", and the semigroup/Lindblad form is the $$\mu\gg1$$ limit of the more general Lévy–Khintchine generator.
- The generator of any such semigroup has a universal "standard form" (GKSL theorem: Gorini–Kossakowski–Sudarshan for finite dimension, Lindblad for bounded generators in separable Hilbert space): a unitary commutator plus a dissipator built from jump (Lindblad) operators $$A_k$$ with non-negative rates $$\gamma_k$$. The book stresses these $$\gamma_k$$ are bath correlation-function rates and act as relaxation/decay rates — they are the microscopic origin of the Sail's $$\lambda$$-type event rates.
- The dissipator term $$A_k\rho A_k^\dagger-\tfrac12\{A_k^\dagger A_k,\rho\}$$ is the only completely-positive, trace-preserving structure allowed; the book derives it by requiring CPTP dynamics and a Hermitian, positive-semidefinite coefficient matrix $$a_{ij}$$, then diagonalizing $$a$$ to get the $$\gamma_k$$. This is the $$\mu\gg1$$ specialization of the Sail's split: the Gaussian-diffusion superoperator $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ corresponds to Lindblad operators that are low-order polynomials in $$x,p$$, while a Lindblad operator that is a coherent displacement $$U_{\Delta p}=\exp[-i\Delta p\,x/\hbar]$$ with a continuum of kicks reproduces the Sail's compound-Poisson jump piece $$\mathcal{J}_P$$.
- Decoherence (Ch.4): for a "measurement-type" interaction that commutes with $$H_S$$ in a preferred basis $$\{|n\rangle\}$$, populations $$P(n)$$ are frozen while off-diagonals decay as $$\langle n|\rho_S|m\rangle\propto e^{\Gamma_{nm}(t)}$$ with $$\Gamma_{nm}(t)\le0$$ (the decoherence function). The bath acts as a probe that reads out $$n$$ without exchanging energy; superpositions across the preferred basis become locally unobservable after a decoherence time $$\tau_D$$.
- For center-of-mass motion the Markovian decoherence master equation is a double commutator in position, $$\dot\rho=-i[H_S,\rho]-\Lambda[x,[x,\rho]]$$ (their $$Z\to x$$). Neglecting the free evolution, off-diagonals in position decay as $$\exp[-\Lambda\,(\Delta x)^2\,t]$$, so the decoherence rate $$\Lambda$$ has units (time)$$^{-1}$$(length)$$^{-2}$$. In every microscopic case (high-$$T$$ Brownian motion, internal-state transitions, scattering) $$\Lambda$$ factorizes as a characteristic rate times a squared wavenumber — structurally the same "rate $$\times$$ momentum-transfer$$^2$$" form that underlies the Sail's $$\dot{\bar n}\propto q^2 S_E(\omega_t)$$ and the jump-size momentum kicks $$\Delta p$$.
- Collective vs. independent coupling: $$N$$ objects coupled to independent baths give maximal decoherence $$\propto N$$; collective coupling gives $$\propto N^2$$ — a system-size amplification noted as relevant to macroscopic superpositions.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\dot\rho=-i[H_0,\rho]+\sum_k \gamma_k\Big(A_k\,\rho\,A_k^\dagger-\tfrac12 A_k^\dagger A_k\,\rho-\tfrac12 \rho\,A_k^\dagger A_k\Big)$$ — the GKSL/Lindblad standard form (their eqn 3.66; their generator $$\mathcal{L}$$, Lindblad operators $$A_k$$, rates $$\gamma_k\ge0$$). This is the Sail's $$\mu\gg1$$ Markovian limit of $$\dot\rho=-i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]+\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$; the dissipator sum plays the role of $$\mathcal{D}_G+\mathcal{J}_P$$ when memory is negligible.
- $$\dot\rho=-i[H_0,\rho]+\sum_{i,j}a_{ij}\Big(F_i\,\rho\,F_j^\dagger-\tfrac12\{F_j^\dagger F_i,\rho\}\Big),\qquad a=(a_{ij})\ \text{Hermitian},\ a\succeq0$$ — the equivalent "first standard form" before diagonalizing (their eqn 3.63); positivity of $$a$$ is the CP condition.
- $$\langle n|\rho|m\rangle(t)=\langle n|\rho|m\rangle(0)\,e^{\Gamma_{nm}(t)},\ \Gamma_{nm}(t)\le0$$ — decoherence function damping off-diagonal coherences in the preferred basis $$\{|n\rangle\}$$ (their eqn 4.11, $$\Gamma_{nm}$$); diagonals $$P(n)$$ stay fixed under ideal (energy-conserving) decoherence.
- $$\dot\rho=-i[H_0,\rho]-\Lambda\,[x,[x,\rho]]$$, with $$H_0=p^2/2m$$ — Markovian position-localization master equation (their eqn 4.69, $$Z=x$$, rate $$\Lambda$$); this is a pure Gaussian-diffusion $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ with Lindblad operator $$\propto x$$.
- $$\rho(t,x,x')=\exp[-\Lambda(\Delta x)^2 t]\,\rho(0,x,x'),\quad \Delta x=x-x',\quad \tau_D=1/[\Lambda(\Delta x)^2]$$ — exponential decay of spatial coherences (their eqn 4.71) and the resulting decoherence time (their eqn 4.74).
- $$\Lambda \sim (\text{rate})\times(\text{wavenumber})^2$$ — generic factorization of the decoherence rate (their eqn 4.75); the "rate" is the Sail's $$\lambda$$-type scattering/event rate and the squared wavenumber encodes the momentum-kick scale, paralleling $$\dot{\bar n}\approx q^2 S_E(\omega_t)/(4m\hbar\omega_t)$$.

**Domain / caveats.** The Lindblad form and the localization master equation hold only in the Markovian (semigroup) regime — bath correlation time $$\tau_c$$ much shorter than system time scales, i.e. the Sail's $$\mu\gg1$$; the book explicitly defers the non-Markovian ($$\mu\lesssim1$$) case to its Part IV. The Lindblad theorem as stated assumes a bounded generator, which fails for the unbounded $$x,p$$ operators of a trapped ion (the book notes that all known physical generators are nonetheless of Lindblad form, or can be cast into it by slight modifications). The text gives only Gaussian-diffusion and Poisson-jump structure; it contains no heavy-tailed / Lévy ($$0<\alpha<2$$) jump-size densities $$\nu(\Delta p)\propto|\Delta p|^{-1-\alpha}$$ — that extension is the Sail's own.

## Collisional decoherence / quantum linear Boltzmann equation (§5.2)

### hornberger2006 — Master equation for a quantum particle in a gas

**Relevance.** Supplies the microscopic, completely-positive collisional master equation that justifies the Sail's Class-II compound-Poisson jump superoperator $$\mathcal{J}_P$$: each gas scattering event imprints a momentum kick $$\Delta p$$ via a displacement operator, with a collision rate fixed by gas density and cross section (our $$\lambda$$) and a kick-size weighting fixed by the scattering amplitude (our $$\nu(\Delta p)$$). The generator's gain–loss (in/out) structure is exactly the jump-plus-loss form the Sail uses.

**Key results (own words).**
- Derives a quantum linear Boltzmann equation for a heavy tracer (mass $$M$$) immersed in a dilute, non-interacting, stationary (momentum-diagonal), spatially uniform ideal gas (mass $$m$$), valid non-perturbatively in the scattering (not restricted to Born approximation). The one essential assumption is Markovianity: collisions are rare, independent two-body events whose rate and effect are each separately meaningful.
- The dissipator has Lindblad form with a continuum of jump operators $$L_{Q,K}=e^{iR\cdot Q/\hbar}\,F(K,P;Q)$$ labelled by momentum transfer $$Q$$ (our $$\Delta p$$). The prefactor $$e^{iR\cdot Q/\hbar}$$ is precisely a momentum-displacement (our $$U_{\Delta p}=\exp[-i\Delta p\, x/\hbar]$$ up to the sign of $$Q$$), so every collision acts as a coherent momentum kick on the tracer.
- The operator-valued amplitude $$F$$ packages all collision physics: the elastic scattering amplitude $$f(p_\text{out},p_\text{in})$$, the gas momentum distribution $$\mu(p)$$ (e.g. Maxwellian), and the gas number density $$n_\text{gas}$$. Crucially, the tracer momentum $$P$$ enters $$f$$ only through its component perpendicular to $$Q$$ and enters $$\mu$$ only through its component parallel to $$Q$$.
- In the momentum representation the generator splits into a gain term (off-diagonal "in" integral over all kicks $$Q$$ that feed coherence $$\rho(P-Q,P'-Q)$$ into $$\rho(P,P')$$) and a loss term ($$-\tfrac12[M^\text{cl}_\text{out}(P)+M^\text{cl}_\text{out}(P')]\rho(P,P')$$). This is the gain/loss (jump + decay) skeleton of $$\mathcal{J}_P$$.
- The total collision rate is an operator $$\Gamma$$ built from gas density, relative speed, and total cross section $$\sigma$$: $$\Gamma=\int dP\,dp\, n_\text{gas}\,v(p,P)\,\sigma\,|P\rangle\langle P|\otimes|p\rangle\langle p|$$. Its expectation gives the mean collision rate — this is the microscopic origin of the Sail's scalar rate $$\lambda$$ (note $$\Gamma$$ is momentum-dependent, so $$\lambda=\langle\Gamma\rangle$$ is its expectation, not a state-independent constant), and $$\lambda\tau$$ counts expected collisions over interrogation time $$\tau$$.
- The diagonal (populations) sector of the master equation reduces exactly to the classical linear Boltzmann equation, $$\partial_t w(P)=\int dQ\,M^\text{cl}_\text{in}(P,Q)\,w(P-Q)-M^\text{cl}_\text{out}(P)\,w(P)$$ — a literal gain–loss master equation whose kick kernel $$M^\text{cl}_\text{in}$$ is set by the differential cross section $$d\sigma/d\Omega=|f|^2$$. This is the classical-limit template for the Sail's $$\nu(\Delta p)$$ jump density.
- Limiting cases recovered: $$m/M\to0$$ gives the Joos–Zeh / Gallis–Fleming (corrected version) pure-decoherence (spatial localization) equation with no dissipation; replacing $$f$$ by its Born approximation gives Vacchini's structure-factor equation. The stationary solution is the thermal Brownian state, and an H-theorem holds.
- The derivation comes from $$S$$-matrix theory: $$\rho'=\mathrm{tr}_\text{gas}\,S(\rho\otimes\rho_\text{gas})S^\dagger$$ with $$S=I+iT$$; unitarity $$S^\dagger S=I$$ produces the gain term ($$T\rho T^\dagger$$) and the anticommutator loss term. The unphysical forward-scattering piece is absorbed into a Hamiltonian (index-of-refraction) shift. A momentum-on-shell replacement rule turns the formal $$T$$-matrix elements into a well-defined kernel.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\dot\rho=-\tfrac{i}{\hbar}[H_0,\rho]+\int d(\Delta p)\Big[U_{\Delta p}\,F\,\rho\,F^\dagger U_{\Delta p}^\dagger-\tfrac12\{F^\dagger F,\rho\}\Big]$$ — Hornberger's Eq. (1) with $$L_{Q,K}=U_{\Delta p}F$$, $$Q\!=\!\Delta p$$; the integral over momentum transfers is the continuous compound-Poisson jump superoperator $$\mathcal{J}_P$$, the anticommutator is the loss term. (Their $$H=P^2/2M$$ is our $$H_0$$; here the tracer is free, not trapped. Since $$U_{\Delta p}$$ is unitary, $$L^\dagger L=F^\dagger F$$.)
- $$L_{\Delta p,K}=\exp[iR\cdot\Delta p/\hbar]\,F(K,P;\Delta p)$$ — each jump is a momentum-kick displacement $$U_{\Delta p}$$ times an amplitude $$F$$; this is the Sail's single-collision jump map for kick $$\Delta p$$.
- $$F\propto \sqrt{n_\text{gas}\,m/m^*}\;f\big(\mathrm{rel}(K,P)_{\perp}-\tfrac{\Delta p}{2},\,\mathrm{rel}(K,P)_{\perp}+\tfrac{\Delta p}{2}\big)\,\sqrt{\mu(\cdots)}$$ — the collision amplitude; the modulus-squared, integrated over the transverse label $$K$$, gives the kick-size density $$\nu(\Delta p)$$ (the Sail's jump-size distribution), with $$f$$ the elastic scattering amplitude and $$\mu$$ the gas momentum distribution. (Their $$f$$ = scattering amplitude; their $$\mu(p)$$ = gas momentum PDF.)
- $$\lambda \;=\; \langle\Gamma\rangle \;=\; \big\langle\,n_\text{gas}\,v\,\sigma(\mathrm{rel}(p,P))\,\big\rangle$$ — mean collision rate from gas density $$\times$$ relative speed $$\times$$ total cross section; identifies the Sail's $$\lambda$$, so $$\lambda\tau$$ is the expected number of momentum kicks during interrogation time $$\tau$$. (Their $$\Gamma$$ is our rate $$\lambda$$ as a momentum-dependent operator, $$\lambda=\langle\Gamma\rangle$$.)
- $$\partial_t w(P)=\int d(\Delta p)\,M^\text{cl}_\text{in}(P,\Delta p)\,w(P-\Delta p)-M^\text{cl}_\text{out}(P)\,w(P)$$ — classical-limit linear Boltzmann (gain–loss) equation for the momentum distribution $$w(P)=\rho(P,P)$$; $$M^\text{cl}_\text{in}\propto n_\text{gas}\,|f|^2$$ is the in-kick rate density, i.e. $$\lambda\,\nu(\Delta p)$$ in the Sail's split, with $$M^\text{cl}_\text{out}(P)=\int d(\Delta p)\,M^\text{cl}_\text{in}(P+\Delta p;\Delta p)$$ the total loss rate.

**Domain / caveats.** Valid only in the dilute, Markovian regime (low gas density, $$\mu=\tau/\tau_c\gg1$$ between independent rare collisions); gas must be ideal, stationary, and uniform (covers thermal Bose/Fermi gases, not liquids). The paper treats a free tracer with continuous momentum, not a trapped ion oscillator — to use it for the Sail's $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ one transcribes the momentum-kick density $$\nu(\Delta p)$$ and rate $$\lambda$$ into the trap's phonon basis; the heavy-mass / Born-limit reductions and the H-theorem (thermal stationary state) bound the regimes where the jump picture stays physical.

### vacchini2009 — Quantum linear Boltzmann equation

**Relevance.** This review supplies the rigorous template for the Sail's master-equation split: a translation-covariant Lindblad generator whose incoherent part is built entirely from displacement (momentum-boost) operators weighted by a single-collision rate and a momentum-transfer probability density. Its compound-Poisson form for collisional decoherence — collisions as Poisson-timed random momentum kicks drawn from a fixed kick distribution — is the concrete physical realization of the Sail's jump superoperator $$\mathcal{J}_P$$, with the total collision rate playing the role of $$\lambda$$ and the kick density playing the role of $$\nu(\Delta p)$$.

**Key results (own words).**
- The quantum linear Boltzmann equation (QLBE) is a Markovian (Lindblad) master equation for a tracer particle in a homogeneous gas: a coherent kinetic-plus-energy-shift part, plus a completely positive incoherent superoperator $$\mathcal{L}$$ that encodes collisions non-perturbatively via the scattering amplitude/cross-section.
- Because the gas is homogeneous, $$\mathcal{L}$$ must be *translation-covariant* — it commutes with momentum-boost translations. Holevo's classification (a quantum Lévy–Khintchine theorem) then forces a specific structure: the only allowed position dependence in the Lindblad operators is through the unitary momentum-boost $$e^{iQ\cdot X/\hbar}$$, with otherwise arbitrary momentum-dependent amplitudes. This is the source's justification for the Sail's generator architecture.
- The general translation-covariant generator splits, à la Lévy–Khintchine, into a *Gaussian (diffusive) part* plus a *Poisson (jump) part*. The bounded case the authors emphasize is purely Poissonian; the diffusive (Gaussian) piece appears as an unbounded limit. This is exactly the Sail's $$\dot\rho = -i[H_0,\rho] + \mathcal{D}_G[\rho] + \mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$ decomposition.
- The jump structure is made explicit in the massive-tracer / pure-decoherence limit: the incoherent dynamics is a stream of random momentum kicks $$\rho\to e^{iQ\cdot X/\hbar}\rho\,e^{-iQ\cdot X/\hbar}$$, occurring at total rate $$\Gamma_{tot}$$, with kick magnitude $$Q$$ drawn from a normalized probability density $$P(Q)$$ (their $$Q$$ = our momentum kick $$\Delta p$$; their $$\Gamma_{tot}$$ = our $$\lambda$$; their $$P(Q)$$ = our normalized jump density $$\nu(\Delta p)$$). The total rate is finite, fixed by the total scattering cross-section.
- The solution of the pure-jump master equation is a *compound-Poisson process*: the number of collisions in time $$t$$ is Poisson with mean $$\Gamma_{tot}\,t$$ (our $$\lambda\tau$$), and each collision multiplies the position off-diagonal by the single-event decoherence function. The off-diagonals decay exponentially while populations are untouched — i.e. localization without dissipation.
- They state the broader claim the Sail leans on: a general translation-covariant decoherence generator yields a *Lévy process* characteristic function (compound-Poisson being one special case), governed by the Lévy–Khintchine formula. This is the formal bridge from the Gaussian/compound-Poisson regimes to the Lévy-type heavy-tailed regime in the Sail's three operational classes.
- In the opposite (heavy tracer, near-equilibrium) limit they expand the kernel to second order in $$X,P$$ and recover a Caldeira–Leggett–type quantum Fokker–Planck / Kramers equation — a quadratic, *Gaussian-diffusion* generator with double commutators in $$X$$ and $$P$$ and microscopically derived friction $$\eta$$. This is the source's $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ counterpart, obtained when collisions are many and small (our $$\lambda\tau\gg1$$ Gaussian regime).
- They verify the jump picture experimentally: fullerene near-field interferometry visibility decays as $$V=V_0 e^{-\Gamma_{tot}t}$$ (room-temperature gas, large-momentum-transfer regime where a single collision destroys coherence), tying the abstract rate $$\lambda=\Gamma_{tot}$$ to a measured collision frequency.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\dot\rho = -\tfrac{i}{\hbar}[H_0 + H_n(P),\rho] + \mathcal{L}[\rho]$$ — full QLBE; coherent kinetic energy $$H_0=P^2/2M$$ plus forward-scattering energy shift $$H_n$$, plus the incoherent collision superoperator $$\mathcal{L}$$ (their Eq. 2.1). Maps onto the Sail's $$-i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G+\mathcal{J}_P$$.
- $$\mathcal{L}[\rho]=\int\! dQ \sum_j \Big( e^{iQ\cdot X/\hbar}\,L_j(P;Q)\,\rho\,L_j^\dagger(P;Q)\,e^{-iQ\cdot X/\hbar} -\tfrac12\{L_j^\dagger L_j,\rho\}\Big)$$ — Holevo's general translation-covariant (bounded/Poissonian) generator: position enters *only* through the momentum-boost $$e^{iQ\cdot X/\hbar}$$, the analog of the Sail's displacement $$U_{\Delta p}=e^{-i\Delta p\,x/\hbar}$$ with $$Q\leftrightarrow\Delta p$$ (sign-convention difference) and the $$Q$$-integral being the continuous jump-size sum (their Eq. 3.6).
- $$\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]=-\lambda\!\int\! d(\Delta p)\;\nu(\Delta p)\,\Big(\rho - U_{\Delta p}^{\dagger}\,\rho\,U_{\Delta p}\Big),\qquad \lambda\equiv\Gamma_{tot},\quad \nu(\Delta p)\equiv P(Q)$$ — pure-jump (massive-tracer) generator: Poisson-rate $$\lambda$$ collisions, each a momentum kick $$\Delta p$$ drawn from normalized density $$\nu$$ (their Eq. 7.5, with $$U_{\Delta p}^\dagger\rho U_{\Delta p}=e^{iQ\cdot X/\hbar}\rho e^{-iQ\cdot X/\hbar}$$).
- $$\nu(\Delta p)=M^{cl}_{in}(\Delta p;\Delta p)\big/\!\int d(\Delta p')\,M^{cl}_{in}(\Delta p';\Delta p')$$ — the normalized jump (momentum-transfer) density, i.e. the classical gain rate normalized by the total rate (their Eq. 7.3); $$M^{cl}_{in}$$ is built from the gas density $$n_{gas}$$, the differential cross-section $$\sigma=|f|^2$$, and the gas momentum distribution $$\mu$$ (their Eq. 2.10).
- $$\lambda=\Gamma_{tot}=\int d(\Delta p')\,M^{cl}_{in}(\Delta p';\Delta p')=M^{cl}_{out}(0)$$ — the total collision rate (event rate) fixed by the total cross-section; finite, so the jump part is genuinely compound-Poisson (their Eq. 7.4). Over interrogation time $$\tau$$ the event count is $$\lambda\tau$$.
- $$\langle X|\rho_t|X'\rangle = e^{-\lambda\,[\,1-\Phi_P(X-X')\,]\,t}\,\langle X|\rho_0|X'\rangle,\qquad \Phi_P(S)=\int d(\Delta p)\,\nu(\Delta p)\,e^{i\,\Delta p\cdot S/\hbar}$$ — exact compound-Poisson decoherence solution; $$\Phi_P$$ (Fourier transform of the kick density) is the single-event characteristic function, and $$e^{-\lambda(1-\Phi_P)t}$$ is the compound-Poisson characteristic function. Population (diagonal) is preserved since $$\Phi_P(0)=1$$ (their Eqs. 7.12–7.16).
- $$\langle X|\rho_t|X'\rangle=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p_n(t)\,\Phi_P^{\,n}(X-X')\,\langle X|\rho_0|X'\rangle$$, with $$p_n(t)=e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^n/n!$$ — explicit jump expansion: Poisson-distributed number of kicks $$n$$ (mean $$\lambda t=\lambda\tau$$), each applying one factor of the single-collision effect (their Eq. 7.17).
- $$\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]=-\eta\sum_j\Big(\tfrac{M}{\hbar^2\beta}[X_j,[X_j,\rho]]+\tfrac{\beta}{16M}[P_j,[P_j,\rho]]+\tfrac{i}{2\hbar}[X_j,\{P_j,\rho\}]\Big)$$ — Gaussian-diffusion (Caldeira–Leggett / Kramers) limit for a heavy tracer near equilibrium: position-decoherence + momentum-diffusion + friction, with microscopic friction $$\eta$$ from the cross-section (their Eq. 5.10). This is the source's realization of the Sail's $$\mathcal{D}_G$$, valid in the many-small-collisions regime.

**Domain / caveats.** The clean jump form $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ (rate $$\lambda$$, kick density $$\nu$$) holds in the *massive-tracer / short-time* limit where energy exchange is negligible and the tracer momentum is a fixed label; the Gaussian $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ limit holds in the opposite *heavy, near-thermal, small-kick* regime ($$M\gg m$$, state close to thermal). The paper's jump density derives from elastic scattering off an ideal non-degenerate gas, so its $$\nu(\Delta p)$$ is cross-section-controlled and has a finite total rate (true compound-Poisson, not a heavy-tailed Lévy law); the genuinely Lévy/heavy-tailed case is only flagged abstractly via the Lévy–Khintchine formula and is not worked out here.

### metzler2000 — The random walk's guide to anomalous diffusion

**Relevance.** This review supplies the textbook foundation for the Sail's Class-III (Lévy-type) heavy-tailed processes: it derives, from a continuous-time random walk (CTRW), how a power-law jump-size density with exponent matching our stability index $$\alpha$$ produces a Lévy-stable propagator and an anomalous (non-linear) mean-square-displacement scaling. It is the source for the $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\propto t^{2/\alpha}$$ signature flagged in §5.3 and for the warning that the literal variance diverges for true Lévy flights.

**Notation correspondence.** The review's jump-length stability index is written $$\mu$$ (rendered "k" in the OCR, range $$0<\mu<2$$, but discussed/illustrated only for $$1<\mu<2$$; the $$0<\mu\le1$$ case is set aside); this is **our heavy-tail / stability index $$\alpha$$**. Their jump length $$x$$ corresponds to a generic increment, i.e. our momentum kick $$\Delta p$$ (or phonon-number kick $$\Delta n$$). Beware a notation clash: the review *also* uses $$\alpha$$ for the *anomalous-diffusion exponent* in the MSD $$\langle x^2\rangle\sim t^{\alpha}$$ — that $$\alpha$$ is **not** ours; it is a derived exponent (in the Lévy-walk Eq. 75 it plays the role of a waiting-time-tail exponent). To avoid confusion I write their MSD/waiting-time exponent as $$\alpha_{\rm MSD}$$ below. Their jump-length pdf is $$\lambda(x)$$ (also written $$j(x)$$ in the general CTRW setup), their waiting-time pdf $$w(t)$$, and their generalized diffusion constant is $$K^{\mu}\equiv\sigma^{\mu}/\tau$$ (superscript $$\mu$$, dimension cm$$^{\mu}$$ s$$^{-1}$$) — **their $$K^{\mu}$$ is our $$K_\alpha$$** below.

**Key results (own words).**
- A CTRW is built from a joint jump pdf factorizing (decoupled case) into a jump-length density and an independent waiting-time density; the nature of the transport is set entirely by whether the characteristic waiting time and the jump-length *variance* are finite or divergent. This is the cleanest microscopic picture behind the Sail's split between diffusive, jump, and heavy-tailed regimes.
- Lévy flights are the case of a *finite* characteristic waiting time but a *divergent* jump-length variance, realized by a jump-length density with a power-law tail. Because the waiting time is well-behaved, the resulting process is explicitly Markovian (the review states this is a consequence of the finite characteristic waiting time) — relevant to the Sail's Markov-accessibility flag $$\mu_{\rm acc}=\tau/\tau_c$$: heavy tails alone do not force non-Markovian dynamics.
- The heavy tail $$\lambda(\Delta p)\sim|\Delta p|^{-1-\alpha}$$ (their Eq. 56) is exactly the Sail's Class-III jump-size density $$\nu(\Delta p)\propto|\Delta p|^{-1-\alpha}$$, $$0<\alpha<2$$. The propagator's Fourier transform is a stretched-exponential-in-$$k$$, i.e. the characteristic function of a centred symmetric Lévy-stable law (their Eq. 60), so the process is generated by Lévy-stable noise — the continuum cousin of the Sail's compound-Poisson jump superoperator $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ in the heavy-tailed limit.
- The real-space propagator decays as a power law $$\sim |\Delta p|^{-1-\alpha}$$ (their Eq. 62). Consequently the genuine second moment **diverges**: $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\to\infty$$ for $$\alpha<2$$ (their Eq. 63). This is the central caveat for the Sail's §5.3 estimators — for a true Lévy tail the sample variance is not a meaningful statistic; one must use the tail exponent (Hill estimator / CCDF) and stability tests rather than $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle$$.
- The Gaussian limit $$\alpha\to2$$ smoothly recovers ordinary diffusion, a finite variance, and the linear MSD $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\sim t$$ (their Eq. 71, via the Fox-function limit of the propagator). This anchors the Sail's continuous family: $$\alpha=2$$ is the Gaussian/diffusive boundary, $$\alpha<2$$ is genuinely heavy-tailed.
- Because the variance diverges, anomalous "diffusion" for Lévy flights is quantified by *fractional* moments $$\langle|\Delta p|^{\delta}\rangle\propto t^{\delta/\alpha}$$ ($$0<\delta<\alpha\le2$$, their Eq. 64, with the exact prefactor in Eq. 69), which are finite, or by the spatial scaling of the propagator: the typical spread grows like $$t^{1/\alpha}$$, so a "width-squared" scales as $$t^{2/\alpha}$$ (their imaginary-box construction, Eq. 72). This is the origin of the Sail's $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\propto t^{2/\alpha}$$ form — it is the *scaling* (propagator-width) statement, valid in distribution, not a convergent ordinary variance.
- For physical particles with finite mass/velocity, instantaneous arbitrarily-long jumps are unphysical; the review replaces Lévy flights by **Lévy walks** (a space-time-coupled CTRW that penalizes long jumps by a travel-time cost, via the delta coupling $$\psi(x,t)=\tfrac12 w(t)\,\delta(|x|-vt)$$), which gives a finite, well-defined MSD. The Lévy-walk MSD exponent differs from the flight scaling and has two regimes (their Eq. 75): $$\langle x^2\rangle\sim t^{\,2+\alpha_{\rm MSD}-\alpha}$$ for a *divergent* mean rest time ($$0<\alpha_{\rm MSD}<1$$), and $$\langle x^2\rangle\sim t^{\,3-\alpha}$$ for $$1<\alpha<2$$ with a *finite* mean rest time. The Sail should note whether its data correspond to instantaneous kicks (flight-like, use $$t^{2/\alpha}$$) or finite-duration excursions (walk-like, different exponent).
- A two-axis "phase diagram" (finite/divergent waiting time × finite/divergent jump variance, Fig. 12) organizes all four regimes: ordinary (Brownian) diffusion, subdiffusion (long rests), Lévy flights (long jumps), and the competition case (long rests *and* long jumps) — a useful schema for classifying the Sail's operational classes.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\sim K_{\alpha_{\rm MSD}}\,t^{\alpha_{\rm MSD}}$$ — the review's general anomalous-MSD power law (their Eq. 5); $$\alpha_{\rm MSD}=1$$ is normal diffusion, $$<1$$ subdiffusion, $$>1$$ superdiffusion, $$\alpha_{\rm MSD}=2$$ ballistic. (Here $$\alpha_{\rm MSD}$$ is their MSD exponent, not our stability index.)
- $$\nu(\Delta p)\sim A\,|\Delta p|^{-1-\alpha}$$ for $$|\Delta p|$$ large, $$0<\alpha<2$$ — heavy-tailed jump-size density (their $$\lambda(x)\sim A_\mu\sigma^{-\mu}|x|^{-1-\mu}$$, Eq. 56, valid for $$1<\mu<2$$), with their Lévy index $$\mu\equiv$$ our $$\alpha$$.
- $$\tilde\lambda(k)=\exp(-\sigma^\alpha|k|^\alpha)\approx 1-\sigma^\alpha|k|^\alpha$$ — characteristic function of the single heavy-tailed jump (their $$\lambda(k)$$, Eq. 55); the $$|k|^\alpha$$ (non-analytic) behaviour is the signature of a Lévy-stable, infinite-variance kick.
- $$P(k,t)=\exp\!\big(-K_\alpha\,t\,|k|^{\alpha}\big)$$ — Fourier transform of the propagator after many jumps (their $$W(k,t)$$, Eq. 60, with $$K_\alpha\equiv$$ their $$K^{\mu}=\sigma^\mu/\tau$$): a centred symmetric Lévy-stable law, our $$P(\Delta p)$$ in the heavy-tailed limit; reduces to the Gaussian $$\exp(-K_2 t k^2)$$ at $$\alpha=2$$.
- $$P(\Delta x,t)\sim \frac{K_\alpha\,t}{|\Delta x|^{1+\alpha}},\qquad \langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle=\int (\Delta x)^2 P\,d(\Delta x)\to\infty\ \ (\alpha<2)$$ — power-law real-space tail (their $$W(x,t)$$, Eq. 62) and the resulting divergence of the ordinary second moment (their Eq. 63): the reason the Sail uses tail-exponent/CCDF/Hill diagnostics rather than a sample variance for Class-III.
- $$\langle|\Delta x|^{\delta}\rangle\propto t^{\delta/\alpha}$$ for $$0<\delta<\alpha\le2$$ — finite fractional moments (their Eq. 64; exact value in Eq. 69); setting the scaling variable $$\xi=\Delta x/t^{1/\alpha}$$ gives the propagator-width scaling whose square is the Sail's $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\propto t^{2/\alpha}$$ (understood as a distributional/scaling statement, exact only as a fractional-moment scaling for $$\delta<\alpha$$).
- $$\langle x^2(t)\rangle\sim t^{\,3-\alpha}$$ ($$1<\alpha<2$$, finite mean rest time) — finite Lévy-*walk* MSD (their Eq. 75, second branch, with their Lévy index $$\mu\equiv$$ our $$\alpha$$), the physically realizable counterpart for massive particles, distinct from the flight scaling; the divergent-mean-rest branch instead gives $$t^{\,2+\alpha_{\rm MSD}-\alpha}$$.

**Domain / caveats.** Results are asymptotic (long-time / diffusion limit $$(k,u)\to(0,0)$$, i.e. $$t\gg\tau$$, small wavenumber), one-dimensional, and assume decoupled, untruncated power-law statistics on a homogeneous support (no quenched disorder, no external force field in this part). Crucially for the Sail: for genuine Lévy flights the ordinary $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle$$ diverges, so the $$t^{2/\alpha}$$ "MSD" is a scaling/fractional-moment statement, not a convergent variance; any finite hardware variance implies a tail cutoff or a Lévy-walk (finite-velocity) mechanism, which changes the exponent. The review does not treat quantum master equations, heating rates, or PSDs — the connection to $$\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$, $$S_E(\omega)$$, and $$\dot{\bar n}$$ is supplied by the Sail, not by this source.

### clauset2009 — Power-law distributions in empirical data

**Relevance.** This is the statistical procedure the Sail invokes for Lévy-type tail-exponent estimation and the §7.4 feasibility gate: maximum-likelihood (Hill) fitting of the tail index, principled selection of the lower cutoff, a Monte-Carlo Kolmogorov–Smirnov goodness-of-fit test that produces a $$p$$-value, likelihood-ratio comparison against competing distributions, and explicit sample-size rules of thumb that justify our "$$\gtrsim 10^3$$–$$10^4$$ tail events" requirement and the detection-only downgrade.

**Key results (own words).**
- **Notation correspondence.** Their scaling parameter (call it $$\alpha_{\mathrm{CSN}}$$) is the exponent of the probability *density*, $$p(x)\propto x^{-\alpha_{\mathrm{CSN}}}$$ above a lower cutoff $$x_{\min}$$. The Sail's jump-size density obeys $$\nu(\Delta p)\propto\lvert\Delta p\rvert^{-1-\alpha}$$, so the correspondence is $$\alpha_{\mathrm{CSN}}=1+\alpha$$ (their density exponent equals our $$1+\alpha$$); equivalently the CCDF/tail exponent is our $$\alpha$$. Their $$x$$ is our tail variable ($$\lvert\Delta p\rvert$$ or $$\lvert\Delta n\rvert$$), their $$x_{\min}$$ is our tail threshold, their $$n_{\mathrm{tail}}$$ is our $$N_{\mathrm{ev}}$$.
- **Fit, then test, then compare.** Their three-step recipe: (1) estimate $$x_{\min}$$ and the exponent by maximum likelihood; (2) test plausibility with a KS-based $$p$$-value (keep the power-law hypothesis only if $$p>0.1$$); (3) compare against alternative laws via a likelihood-ratio test. This maps directly onto the Sail's §5.3/§7.4 workflow.
- **MLE = Hill estimator.** The continuous maximum-likelihood exponent is the Hill estimator; it is asymptotically normal and consistent. They show on synthetic data that the popular log–log-histogram-slope fit (ordinary least squares on the binned PDF, with or without logarithmic bins) is badly and systematically biased, while a least-squares fit to the CDF/rank-frequency curve is biased in the discrete case though it does reasonably well in the continuous case. Crucially, in every biased case the reported error bar flags nothing — the experimenter gets no signal that the estimate is wrong — so they recommend the MLE in all circumstances.
- **Discrete case.** For integer-valued data the MLE has no closed form; one solves a transcendental equation in (Hurwitz/generalized) zeta functions or maximizes the log-likelihood numerically. A rounded-continuum approximation (shift $$x_{\min}\to x_{\min}-\tfrac12$$) is accurate to ~1% once $$x_{\min}\gtrsim 6$$.
- **Choosing $$x_{\min}$$ by minimizing KS distance.** Their preferred, distribution-agnostic method picks the cutoff that minimizes the KS distance $$D$$ between the empirical CCDF and the best-fit power-law CCDF above the cutoff. Too low a cutoff biases the exponent (fitting non-power-law data); too high a cutoff throws away legitimate data and inflates both the statistical error and the finite-size bias. A Bayesian/BIC alternative exists for discrete data but tends to underestimate the cutoff and does not generalize to continuous data.
- **Monte-Carlo $$p$$-value.** The analytic KS $$p$$-value formula cannot be used because the model is itself fitted to each dataset (the reference distribution varies set to set). Instead they bootstrap: fit the data, then generate many semiparametric synthetic datasets (power-law tail above $$x_{\min}$$, resampled empirical body below), refit each, and define $$p$$ as the fraction of synthetic KS distances exceeding the observed one. To reach accuracy $$\epsilon$$ in $$p$$ one needs $$\gtrsim \tfrac14\epsilon^{-2}$$ synthetic sets (e.g. ~2500 for two-digit accuracy).
- **Likelihood-ratio test for alternatives.** A high power-law $$p$$-value does not prove the power law — log-normal, exponential, stretched-exponential, or power-law-with-cutoff may fit as well or better. The (Vuong) log-likelihood ratio $$R$$ between two non-nested candidates is asymptotically normal; its sign says which model is favored, and a companion $$p$$-value (from $$R/\sigma$$) says whether that sign is trustworthy or just a fluctuation. Nested cases (power law vs. power-law-with-cutoff) instead use a test based on the $$\chi^2$$ distribution of $$R$$.
- **Sample-size requirements (the feasibility-gate basis).** Exponent bias decays as $$O(1/n)$$ while statistical error decays as $$O(1/\sqrt n)$$; they suggest $$n\gtrsim 50$$ as a bare minimum for the exponent and, crucially, $$\gtrsim 1000$$ observations *in the tail* ($$n_{\mathrm{tail}}$$, i.e. above $$x_{\min}$$) for reliable $$x_{\min}$$ and hypothesis testing. Larger $$x_{\min}$$ means fewer tail points, so more total data are needed to reject a wrong model. Small datasets cannot rule out (or confirm) heavy tails — directly motivating the Sail's "downgrade to detection-only."
- **Goodness-of-fit statistic choice.** They use the plain KS statistic but note reweighted-KS, Kuiper, and Anderson–Darling alternatives (reweighted-KS and Kuiper give results very similar to plain KS). The Anderson–Darling statistic, which weights tail deviations more heavily, they find to be highly conservative in this application: it pushes $$\hat x_{\min}$$ up by an order of magnitude or more. That conservatism may be acceptable only when many tail samples remain, otherwise it sacrifices too many tail observations and inflates the exponent error — consistent with the Sail's recommendation to use Anderson–Darling for the heavy-tail screen only at large $$N$$.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\nu(\Delta p)\propto\lvert\Delta p\rvert^{-1-\alpha}$$, with their density exponent $$\alpha_{\mathrm{CSN}}=1+\alpha$$ — the tail law the estimator targets; equivalently the CCDF exponent is our stability index $$\alpha$$.
- $$\hat\alpha = \Big[\,n\Big(\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln\frac{x_i}{x_{\min}}\Big)^{-1}\Big]\quad\text{(their }\hat\alpha_{\mathrm{CSN}}=1+\hat\alpha\text{)}$$ — continuous maximum-likelihood / Hill estimator from the $$n=N_{\mathrm{ev}}$$ tail samples $$x_i\ge x_{\min}$$.
- $$\sigma_{\hat\alpha}=\frac{\hat\alpha_{\mathrm{CSN}}-1}{\sqrt{n}}+O(1/n)=\frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{n}}+O(1/n)$$ — standard error of the exponent, decaying as $$1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{ev}}}$$; this is what the feasibility gate must keep small.
- $$D=\max_{x\ge x_{\min}}\big|S(x)-P(x)\big|,\qquad \hat x_{\min}=\arg\min_{x_{\min}} D$$ — KS distance between empirical CCDF $$S$$ and fitted power-law CCDF $$P$$; cutoff chosen to minimize it.
- $$p \;=\; \mathrm{erfc}\!\big(\,|R|/\sqrt{2 n \sigma}\,\big),\qquad R=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[\ln p_1(x_i)-\ln p_2(x_i)\big]$$ — likelihood-ratio test: $$R$$ is the log-likelihood ratio of candidate models 1 vs. 2, $$\sigma$$ the per-point dispersion of its summands; small $$p$$ means the sign of $$R$$ (which model wins) is significant.
- $$N_{\mathrm{synth}}\gtrsim \tfrac14\epsilon^{-2}$$ — number of Monte-Carlo surrogate datasets needed for a $$p$$-value accurate to $$\pm\epsilon$$.

**Domain / caveats.** The method is asymptotic: estimates are only guaranteed unbiased as $$N_{\mathrm{ev}}\to\infty$$, and a large $$p$$-value never proves a power law — it only fails to reject it, and with too little tail data even non-power-law sources pass. This is exactly why the Sail's §7.4 gate refuses to publish a point estimate of $$\alpha$$ unless $$N_{\mathrm{ev}}\gtrsim 10^3$$–$$10^4$$ tail events are achievable within the drift-free (stationary) acquisition window, and otherwise reports "heavy tails detected" only.

### hill1975 — A simple general approach to inference about the tail of a distribution

**Relevance.** Source of the Hill estimator that the Sail uses to read off the heavy-tail stability index from the upper-tail order statistics of a jump-size (or phonon-number) sample, when only the tail — not the full distribution — is assumed to be of power-law (Zipf/Pareto) form. It fixes the estimator formula, the tail-index/$$\alpha$$ correspondence, and the conditional sampling distribution used for error bars and consistency.

**Key results (own words).**
- Hill assumes nothing about the global distribution, only that the upper tail is of Zipf/Pareto type: the complementary CDF decays as a power law beyond some threshold. In Sail terms, this is exactly the assumed jump-size CCDF $$\bar\nu(\Delta p)\propto\lvert\Delta p\rvert^{-\alpha}$$ that defines the Lévy class.
- His tail index is denoted $$\alpha$$ and is identical to the Sail stability index $$\alpha$$ (their CCDF exponent), so no inversion is needed here — Hill's $$\alpha$$ *is* our $$\alpha$$ (their $$C$$ is a tail amplitude with no Sail counterpart). The estimator targets $$\alpha$$ directly, not $$\gamma=1/\alpha$$.
- The estimator is built only from the largest $$r+1$$ order statistics: order the sample descending, take the top $$r+1$$ values, and form a weighted log-spacing average. Inference about $$\alpha$$ alone needs only ratios of consecutive top order statistics, so the exact threshold $$D$$ (where the power law starts) need not be known.
- The estimator is a conditional maximum-likelihood estimate: conditioning on the extreme order statistics reduces the nonparametric tail problem to a one-parameter exponential/gamma likelihood for $$\alpha$$, via the Rényi representation of order statistics.
- Conditional sampling distribution: the (CML) estimate has the law of $$r/X(r)$$ with $$X(r)$$ a Gamma$$(r)$$ variate, giving a slight upward bias $$E[\hat\alpha]=r\alpha/(r-1)$$ and variance $$\propto 1/r$$ — so the estimator is consistent and asymptotically unbiased as the number of retained upper-tail samples $$r\to\infty$$.
- Choosing $$r$$ (how deep into the tail to go) is a bias–variance trade-off done by data analysis, not theory: the rescaled log-spacings $$iV_i$$ with $$V_i=\ln[Y^{(i)}/Y^{(i+1)}]$$ should look like i.i.d. Exponential($$\alpha$$) variates while the power law holds; one increases $$r$$ and applies a goodness-of-fit (chi-square) test, stopping when systematic departures appear. This is the role node 9 plays in the Sail's Class-III pipeline.
- Empirically (city-size data, $$r=29$$) the relative scatter of the estimate was about $$0.20\,\alpha$$, i.e. order-20% statistical uncertainty for a few dozen tail points.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\hat\alpha \;=\; \frac{r+1}{\sum_{i=1}^{r}\ln Y^{(i)} \;-\; r\,\ln Y^{(r+1)}}\;=\;\Big[\tfrac{1}{r+1}\!\sum_{i=1}^{r}\ln\!\frac{Y^{(i)}}{Y^{(r+1)}}\Big]^{-1}$$ — the Hill estimator of the Sail stability index $$\alpha$$, from the $$r+1$$ largest descending order statistics $$Y^{(1)}\ge\cdots\ge Y^{(r+1)}$$ of a heavy-tailed sample (here $$Y$$ are the magnitudes $$\lvert\Delta p\rvert$$ or $$\lvert\Delta n\rvert$$); the denominator is the sum of log-excesses above the $$(r+1)$$th value. (Hill's eq. 3.1, which keeps the $$r+1$$ normalization rather than the modern $$r$$; his $$\alpha,C$$ map to our $$\alpha$$ and an unused tail amplitude.)
- $$V_i = \ln\!\big[Y^{(i)}/Y^{(i+1)}\big]$$, and $$i\,V_i \stackrel{\text{approx}}{\sim}\text{Exp}(\alpha)$$ — the rescaled log-spacings that should be i.i.d. exponential with rate $$\alpha$$ while the power-law tail holds; their goodness-of-fit is the diagnostic for the cutoff index $$r$$.
- $$E[\hat\alpha] = \dfrac{r\,\alpha}{r-1}\;(r>1),\qquad \mathrm{Var}[\hat\alpha] = \dfrac{(r\,\alpha)^2}{(r-1)^2(r-2)}\;(r>2)$$ — conditional mean and variance (from $$\hat\alpha\sim r/\mathrm{Gamma}(r)$$), showing the small upward bias and $$O(1/r)$$ variance that vanish as more tail points are used.

**Domain / caveats.** Valid only for a genuinely power-law (Zipf/Pareto) upper tail; the Sail restricts this to its Lévy class with $$0<\alpha<2$$, though Hill himself imposes only $$\alpha>0$$. It estimates the tail exponent and says nothing about the bulk. Accuracy hinges on the choice of $$r$$: too few top points inflate variance, too many include non-tail samples and bias the estimate — Hill gives no closed-form optimal $$r$$, only the exponential-spacing (chi-square) goodness-of-fit guide, so the Sail must report $$\hat\alpha$$ together with an $$r$$-stability and Anderson–Darling check (a Sail-side addition beyond Hill's own chi-square test) rather than a single number.

## Lévy–Khintchine background (companion for the unheld Sato monograph)

### applebaum2004 — Lévy Processes (Notices of the AMS, expository companion)

**Relevance.** Free-OA stand-in for the Sato monograph: it states the Lévy–Khintchine formula and the Lévy–Itô path decomposition, which together give the rigorous "Gaussian part + jump part" split underpinning the Sail's three-class hypothesis ($$\mathcal{D}_G$$ vs. $$\mathcal{J}_P$$) and the heavy-tailed Lévy-type class. The explicit generator (its Eq. 0.4) is the classical analogue of our master-equation generator split.

**Key results (own words).**
- A Lévy process is defined by independent, stationary increments, starting at zero (w.p.1), with stochastic continuity. With this regularity the paths can be taken right-continuous with left limits, so the process moves continuously except at a countable set of jumps. Each marginal $$X(t)$$ is infinitely divisible — exactly the property that lets a single noise process be built up from arbitrarily many i.i.d. pieces.
- The characteristic function factorizes as $$\phi_t(u)=e^{t\eta(u)}$$, so the whole process is encoded in one characteristic exponent $$\eta(u)$$ and a "characteristics" triple $$(b,a,\nu)$$: a drift vector $$b$$, a non-negative-definite symmetric diffusion matrix $$a$$, and a Lévy (jump-size) measure $$\nu$$. In our language $$a$$ feeds $$\mathcal{D}_G$$ and $$\nu$$ feeds $$\mathcal{J}_P$$; the source's $$\nu(dy)$$ is our jump density $$\nu(\Delta p)\,d(\Delta p)$$ (their increment variable $$y$$ is our momentum kick $$\Delta p$$).
- The two "extreme" building blocks are Brownian motion (characteristics $$(0,a,0)$$, purely Gaussian) and the Poisson process (characteristics $$(0,0,\lambda\delta_1)$$, pure unit jumps at rate $$\lambda$$). The general process lives between them as an interior point of the convex cone of exponents; the source's intensity $$\lambda$$ is our event rate $$\lambda$$.
- The compound Poisson process sums i.i.d. jumps $$Y_j$$ (drawn from law $$q$$) at Poisson-timed instants — this is precisely the operational picture behind our compound-Poisson class ($$\mathcal{J}_P$$ with $$\lambda\tau\sim1\dots\ll1$$); their $$Y_j$$ is our $$\Delta p$$ and their $$q$$ is our normalized $$\nu$$ (so $$\nu=\lambda q$$).
- The Lévy measure need not be finite: there can be infinitely many "small" jumps. De Finetti's naive guess (a finite jump measure $$\lambda q$$) fails because, once it is replaced by a $$\sigma$$-finite Lévy measure $$\nu$$, the integrand $$(e^{iu\cdot y}-1)$$ need not be $$\nu$$-integrable; one must subtract the linear term $$iu\cdot y\,\mathbf{1}_{\|y\|<1}$$ (compensation) to make the small-jump contribution converge. This compensation is exactly why our $$\mathcal{J}_P$$ uses *compensated* Poisson kicks.
- Stable Lévy processes are the heavy-tailed interior case: jump measure $$\nu(dx)=C|x|^{-\alpha-1-(d-1)}dx$$ with $$0<\alpha<2$$ (in 1D, $$\propto|x|^{-1-\alpha}$$). Their index of stability $$\alpha$$ is our stability index $$\alpha$$; for $$\alpha<2$$ the variance is infinite (and the mean too if $$\alpha\le1$$) and the tail decays as a power law $$P(X>y)\sim y^{-\alpha}$$ rather than exponentially — this is the Sail's Lévy-type class.
- Stable processes are self-similar: $$X(ct)$$ has the same law as $$c^{1/\alpha}X(t)$$, which is the source's version of our anomalous-diffusion scaling $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\propto t^{2/\alpha}$$ (their length scales as $$t^{1/\alpha}$$). The $$\alpha=1$$ Cauchy process is the canonical heavy-tailed example.
- The Lévy–Itô decomposition splits any Lévy path into a continuous (drift + Brownian) part plus a compensated sum of small jumps plus a raw sum of large jumps. Crucially: the drift $$b$$ is *not* the mean in general, and any failure of $$X(t)$$ to have an $$n$$-th moment is caused entirely by the large-jump term — directly relevant to why heavy-tailed kicks break ordinary cumulant ($$\kappa_3,\kappa_4$$) descriptions.
- The infinitesimal generator $$A$$ of the associated Markov (Feller) semigroup splits into a second-order (diffusion) piece, a first-order (drift) piece, and a non-local integral (jump) piece — the classical scalar template for our operator-valued $$\dot\rho = -i[H_0,\rho]+\mathcal{D}_G[\rho]+\mathcal{J}_P[\rho]$$. Rotationally invariant $$\alpha$$-stable noise is generated by the fractional Laplacian $$-(-\Delta)^{\alpha/2}$$, the spatial signature of the Lévy-type class.

**Formulas in our notation.**
- $$\phi_t(u)=e^{t\,\eta(u)},\qquad \eta(u)=i\,b\!\cdot\!u-\tfrac{1}{2}u\!\cdot\!a\,u+\!\!\int_{\Delta p\neq0}\!\!\big[e^{\,i u\,\Delta p}-1-i u\,\Delta p\,\mathbf{1}_{|\Delta p|<1}\big]\,\nu(\Delta p)\,d(\Delta p)$$ — the Lévy–Khintchine formula: the characteristic exponent of an infinitely divisible noise = drift $$b$$ + Gaussian/diffusion part $$a$$ (→ $$\mathcal{D}_G$$) + compensated jump part with Lévy measure $$\nu(\Delta p)$$ (→ $$\mathcal{J}_P$$). (Source variable $$y\to\Delta p$$.)
- $$X(t)=b\,t+B_a(t)+\!\!\int_{|\Delta p|<1}\!\!\Delta p\,\tilde N(t,d\Delta p)+\!\!\int_{|\Delta p|\ge1}\!\!\Delta p\,N(t,d\Delta p)$$ — the Lévy–Itô decomposition: drift + Brownian diffusion + compensated small jumps + large jumps; $$\tilde N=N-t\nu$$ is the compensated Poisson random measure, $$N(t,A)$$ counts jumps landing in set $$A$$ (a Poisson process of intensity $$\nu(A)$$).
- $$\nu(\Delta p)\propto |\Delta p|^{-1-\alpha},\ 0<\alpha<2$$ — the heavy-tailed stable Lévy measure defining our Lévy-type class; finite variance only at the Gaussian boundary $$\alpha=2$$.
- $$X(ct)\overset{d}{=}c^{1/\alpha}X(t)$$ — self-similarity of stable noise, i.e. the anomalous spreading $$\langle(\Delta x)^2\rangle\propto t^{2/\alpha}$$ used in the Sail.
- $$(Af)(x)=\tfrac12\sum_{ij}a_{ij}\partial_i\partial_j f+\sum_i b_i\partial_i f+\int\!\big[f(x{+}\Delta p)-f(x)-\Delta p\!\cdot\!\nabla f\,\mathbf{1}_{|\Delta p|<1}\big]\nu(\Delta p)\,d(\Delta p)$$ — the generator's diffusion + drift + jump split, the scalar prototype of $$\mathcal{D}_G+\mathcal{J}_P$$; $$\alpha$$-stable noise → $$A=-(-\Delta)^{\alpha/2}$$.

**Domain / caveats.** This is a classical (commuting, scalar-valued) expository treatment on $$\mathbb{R}^d$$, not the quantum master-equation setting; it supplies the structural Gaussian-vs-jump-vs-heavy-tail taxonomy but says nothing about $$S_E(\omega)$$, $$\dot{\bar n}$$, the displacement operators $$U_{\Delta p}$$, or the Markov-accessibility scalar $$\mu=\tau/\tau_c$$ — those mappings are ours. It also assumes stationary, independent increments (true Lévy/Markov noise), which excludes correlated or non-stationary bath dynamics ($$\mu\lesssim1$$).
